## ✝️ CHRISTOLOGICAL ROOT
*The foundation before the study begins*

### 🔎 What Jesus Said/Did

> *"Think not that I am come to destroy the law, or the prophets: I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil."* — Matthew 5:17 (KJV)

> *"Blessed are the pure in heart: for they shall see God."* — Matthew 5:8 (KJV)

> *"Ye have heard that it was said by them of old time… But I say unto you…"* — Matthew 5:21–22 (KJV, repeated six times)

> *"Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect."* — Matthew 5:48 (KJV)

### 🔗 The Bridge

Matthew 5 is Jesus' throne speech — His inaugural address as the King of the Kingdom of Heaven. He does not arrive as a commentator on Moses; He arrives as the Lawgiver Himself revealing what the law always meant. The six antitheses (*"Ye have heard… but I say unto you"*) are not replacements — they are *exposés*: every Mosaic command was targeting the heart's orientation all along, and the Pharisees had settled for the surface. This is the Christological root of everything Paul teaches about sanctification, holiness, and the new covenant: Jesus established that the standard reaches the heart (v. 8), that He came to *fill the law to its fullness* (v. 17), and that the goal is a completeness of character that mirrors the Father (v. 48). Without Matthew 5, Paul's commands in Romans 8 and 1 Thessalonians 4 have no Christological ground to stand on.

### ⚠️ What Gets Lost Without This Root

Without Matthew 5 as the Christological root, teachers reduce holiness to a behavior checklist — and miss that Jesus is the *only* one who both sets and meets the standard, and then *gives* that righteousness to those united to Him.

🌍 **Missiological Note:** The Sermon on the Mount is delivered to disciples but within earshot of *"great multitudes"* (Matt 4:25; 5:1). The beatitudes describe a community so visibly different — merciful, pure, peacemaking — that they become a light to all nations (v. 14). Matthew 5 is not just personal ethics; it is the *missionary identity* of the Kingdom community. The church's holiness is its witness.

## 🟢 TIER 1 — THE FOUNDATION
*New believers, youth, anyone starting from zero*

---

### 📌 Core Concept

Imagine the best teacher you ever had — one who didn't just give you the rules, but showed you what the rules were *really for*. That's what Jesus does in Matthew 5. The religious leaders of His day had taken God's law and turned it into a checklist — don't murder, don't commit adultery, keep your promises. Jesus looks at the checklist and says: *"You've missed the point entirely."* The law was never just about your hands — it was always about your heart. Don't just avoid murder; deal with your anger. Don't just avoid adultery; deal with your lust. Don't just look holy; *be* holy — from the inside out. And the reason this matters: the pure in heart are the ones who will one day see God face to face.

### 🪞 Analogy

Think of an iceberg. The Pharisees were policing the tip — the 10% visible above water. Jesus says: I see the 90% underneath. The visible tip — your behavior — is shaped by the invisible mass below — your desires, your anger, your secret motivations, your cultivated thoughts. Religion that only deals with the tip produces people who look good from a distance. Jesus is after the whole iceberg. He wants the underwater part — the heart — transformed. And He's the only one who can do it.

## 🟡 TIER 2 — THE BUILDER
*Growing believers, small group leaders, Sunday school teachers*

---

### 📌 Deeper Dive

Here is what almost every reader of Matthew 5 misses: Jesus is not making the law harder. He is revealing what the law always was — and in doing so, He is making the case that no one can meet it without a new heart. That is the theological genius of the Sermon on the Mount. It is not a self-improvement program. It is the diagnosis that drives you to the Physician.

Matthew writes primarily for a Jewish-Christian audience in the late 50s–80s AD, likely in Antioch or a Diaspora context. He structures his Gospel around five major discourses — a deliberate parallel to the five books of Moses. The Sermon on the Mount (chapters 5–7) is the first and greatest discourse. By opening with Jesus ascending a mountain (v. 1) and teaching with sovereign authority, Matthew is presenting Jesus as the new Moses — not a commentator on Torah but its author now revealing its full intent.

The Beatitudes (vv. 3–12) are not a to-do list. They are a portrait — a description of who Kingdom citizens are when the Spirit has done His work. They move from the posture of *reception* (*poor in spirit, mournful, meek* — the empty-handed) to the posture of *action* (*merciful, pure, peacemaking* — the transformed) to the posture of *endurance* (*persecuted* — the tested). The person described across all eight beatitudes is, perfectly, Jesus Himself. These are not eight separate virtues to achieve — they are one integrated character that flows from genuine union with the King.

The six antitheses (vv. 21–48) are where the Sermon becomes most confrontational. Each one takes a Mosaic command and exposes the heart-level intent the Pharisees had obscured. The pattern is not *"Moses said X, but Jesus says not-X"* — it is *"you heard the surface reading; now hear the depth."* Anger is the root of murder. Lust is the beginning of adultery. Oath-taking signals an integrity problem the oath is trying to compensate for. Jesus is conducting a heart audit on every major domain of human relationship — and no one passes.

The climax — *"Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect"* (v. 48) — is not the command to achieve sinless perfection. The Greek *teleios* means *complete, whole, arrived at its telos.* The perfection Jesus commands is patterned after the Father's *completeness of love* (vv. 44–45 — love without discrimination, as God sends rain on just and unjust). It is both an ethical standard for now and an eschatological destination — what we will fully be when we see Him (1 John 3:2).

### 🔤 Original Language Insight — *Makarios* (Blessed)

**Word:** *μακάριος* — **makarios**
**Transliteration:** mak-AR-ee-os
**Definition:** Happy, blessed, fortunate — but in NT usage, the deep objective blessedness of one who is in right relationship with God; not an emotional feeling but a divine declaration of status

**The word picture:** In classical Greek, *makarios* was used of the gods — the blessed ones, the fortunate ones who lived above earthly trouble. The LXX uses it to translate the Hebrew *'ashre* — found in Ps 1:1 (*"Blessed is the man"*) and throughout the Psalms — the blessedness of the one whose life is aligned with God's order.

**Why it matters in Matt 5:** Jesus opens with eight *makarios* declarations — not commands but *announcements*. He is not saying *"do these things to become blessed."* He is saying *"these people — the poor in spirit, the mourning, the meek — are already the blessed ones in the Kingdom's economy."* This is the indicative before the imperative: the Beatitudes describe Kingdom citizens, not achievement targets. They are declarations of identity, not instructions for merit.

**Contrast with the world's *makarios*:** The world declares blessed the rich, the powerful, the celebrated, the comfortable. Jesus inverts every category — the poor, the mourning, the persecuted are the truly *makarios*. This is not sentimentality; it is a sovereign reordering of what reality actually looks like in the Kingdom of God.

---

### ⚠️ Common Misconception

**Myth:** *"The Sermon on the Mount is Jesus' ethical teaching — a guide for living a good life, applicable to anyone regardless of faith."*

**Truth:** The Sermon is not general ethics — it is Kingdom ethics, addressed to disciples (v. 1 — *"his disciples came unto him"*), describing the character of those who have already entered the Kingdom through the new birth. It presupposes regeneration. The command to love enemies (v. 44) and the standard of *teleios* perfection (v. 48) are not achievable by moral effort — they require the indwelling Spirit (John 14:16–17). Gandhi famously admired the Sermon and tried to practice it. The Sermon itself reveals why that project is impossible without the Giver of the Sermon living within. It is not a program for the willing; it is a portrait of the transformed.

## 🔴 TIER 3 — THE MASTERCLASS
*Pastors, teachers, theology students, deep divers*

---

### 📌 Theological Framework

Matthew 5 sits at the convergence of five major theological loci:

**1. Christology — The New Moses and the Divine Lawgiver**
Matthew deliberately structures the Sermon to present Jesus as the new Moses: He ascends a mountain (v. 1; cf. Exod 19:3), teaches with sovereign authority (*"But I say unto you"* — not *"the Lord says"* or *"it is written"*), and delivers the law of the Kingdom. But Jesus exceeds Moses categorically — Moses received the law; Jesus *gives* it from His own authority. The six antitheses demonstrate this: Jesus does not cite precedent but speaks as the Lawgiver. His *"But I say unto you"* is the voice of the one who spoke from Sinai, now speaking face to face. This is Matthew's highest Christological claim embedded in the teaching narrative.

**2. Hamartiology — The Heart as the Site of Sin**
The six antitheses collectively constitute the most comprehensive NT statement about sin's *location.* Jesus does not merely deepen the law's demands — He relocates the moral arena from the public to the interior. Murder's root is anger and contempt (v. 22); adultery's root is cultivated desire (v. 28); oath-breaking's root is a compromised integrity (v. 37); violence's root is a retaliatory heart (v. 39); hatred's root is a love that only extends to the lovable (v. 43). Every antithesis is a diagnostic: the behavior is the symptom; the heart is the disease. This maps precisely onto Mark 7:21–23 — both texts together form the NT's definitive hamartiology.

**3. Law and Gospel — Fulfillment, Not Abolition**
Matthew 5:17–20 is the most important NT text on the relationship between Jesus and the Mosaic law. *Plēroō* (fulfil) operates on multiple levels: (a) prophetic fulfillment — Jesus brings to completion what the OT pointed toward; (b) hermeneutical fulfillment — He reveals the law's deepest intent; (c) eschatological fulfillment — He embodies the law's telos in His own person and then imparts that righteousness to His people (Rom 5:19; 8:3–4). The Pharisees' *"righteousness"* (v. 20) was external compliance; the Kingdom righteousness that exceeds it is the transformed heart — which only the new covenant can produce (Jer 31:33; Ezek 36:27).

**4. Sanctification — Progressive Movement Toward Teleios**
Matthew 5:48 (*"Be ye therefore perfect"*) is the telos statement of the entire chapter and — read canonically — of the entire sanctification process. *Teleios* (complete, whole, arrived at the intended goal) describes simultaneously: the ethical standard for present life (love without discrimination, vv. 44–45), the character of mature believers (1 Cor 2:6; Phil 3:15), and the eschatological destination of glorification (Phil 3:12; 1 John 3:2). Progressive sanctification is the movement between where the believer is and where v. 48 points — enabled by the Spirit (1 Thess 4:8), grounded in union with Christ (1 Cor 1:30), and completed at the *parousia*.

**5. Missiology — The Community as Witness**
Salt and light (vv. 13–16) embed a missiological theology within the ethical teaching. The community that embodies the Beatitudes and lives the antitheses is, by its very existence, a witness. *"A city that is set on an hill cannot be hid"* (v. 14) — the visibility is not achieved by strategy but by character. This is the NT's most compressed statement of the church's missional identity: holiness and witness are not separate categories. The pure community is the missionary community.

### 📖 Key Verses with Exegetical Notes (KJV)

| Verse | KJV Text | Exegetical Note |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| Matt 5:3 | *"Blessed are the poor in spirit: for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."* | *"Poor in spirit"* — *ptōchoi tō pneumati* — *ptōchos* is the destitute poor, not merely the economically modest (*penēs*). Spiritual destitution — the recognition of absolute bankruptcy before God — is the posture that receives the Kingdom. This is the anthropological ground of grace: the Kingdom is not earned, it is received by the empty-handed. The present tense *"is"* (not *"will be"*) signals the already-inaugurated Kingdom. |
| Matt 5:8 | *"Blessed are the pure in heart: for they shall see God."* | *"Pure"* — *katharoi* — plural of *katharos*: uncontaminated, unmixed, genuine. The *kardia* (heart) is the center of the whole person — will, intellect, desire, orientation (see the full Hebrew *lēb* background). *"Shall see God"* — *theōn opsontai* — future: the eschatological beatific vision. This is simultaneously a present process (the pure in heart perceive God's reality now, cf. John 14:21) and a future completion (1 John 3:2). |
| Matt 5:17 | *"I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil."* | *"Destroy"* — *katalusai* — to demolish, to dissolve, to make null; *"fulfil"* — *plērōsai* — aorist infinitive of *plēroō*: to fill to the full, to bring to its intended completeness. The contrast is total: demolition vs. completion. Jesus is not abrogating one jot or tittle (v. 18 — *iōta* = yod, the smallest Hebrew letter; *keraia* = a serif stroke) but driving every letter to its ultimate depth. |
| Matt 5:20 | *"Except your righteousness shall exceed the righteousness of the scribes and Pharisees, ye shall in no case enter into the kingdom of heaven."* | *"Exceed"* — *perisseuō* — to overflow, to abound beyond measure. This is qualitative, not quantitative. The Pharisees' righteousness was extensive in scope — meticulous tithing, ritual purity, Sabbath observance. Jesus is not saying *do more rules*; He is saying *you need a different kind of righteousness entirely* — one that flows from a transformed heart rather than an impressive performance. |
| Matt 5:22 | *"Whosoever is angry with his brother without a cause shall be in danger of the judgment."* | *"Angry"* — *orgizomenos* — present participle: one who is *harboring* anger, not merely one who felt anger momentarily. *"Without a cause"* (*eikē*) is absent in some manuscripts (including Sinaiticus and Vaticanus) — the stronger reading may be simply *"whosoever is angry with his brother"* — making the indictment absolute. *"Raca"* (v. 22) — Aramaic term of contempt: *empty-head.* *"Fool"* (*mōre*) — moral contempt. Three escalating levels: anger → verbal contempt → moral dismissal — each with escalating judgment. |
| Matt 5:28 | *"Whosoever looketh on a woman to lust after her hath committed adultery with her already in his heart."* | *"To lust"* — *pros to epithumēsai* — infinitive of purpose: looking *in order to desire.* The grammatical construction specifies intentional, cultivated desire — not involuntary attraction. The *kardia* (heart) is again the site of judgment — not the action but the *intention behind the gaze.* This relocates the entire domain of sexual ethics from the bedroom to the inner life. |
| Matt 5:44 | *"Love your enemies, bless them that curse you, do good to them that hate you, and pray for them which despitefully use you, and persecute you."* | *"Love"* — *agapate* — present imperative: keep on loving. *Agapē* here is not affection (*philia*) or desire (*erōs*) but the willed, active commitment to another's good regardless of feeling or return. The fourfold expansion — bless, do good, pray, love — shows that *agapē* is not passive but comprehensive and active. The motivation (v. 45) — *"that ye may be the children of your Father"* — grounds the command in identity: you love like the Father because you *are* His children. |
| Matt 5:48 | *"Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect."* | *"Perfect"* — *teleios* — complete, whole, arrived at its intended *telos.* The pattern (*"even as your Father"*) indicates the perfection in view is specifically the Father's *completeness of love* demonstrated in v. 45 — love without discrimination. *Teleios* in Phil 3:12–15 shows Paul simultaneously denying having attained it and claiming it as the present orientation of mature believers — the word holds both the present trajectory and the eschatological destination in tension. |

### 📚 Scholarly Insight

- **D.A. Carson** (*The Sermon on the Mount*, 1978): On v. 17 — Carson distinguishes three senses of *plēroō*: (1) bringing OT prophecy to fulfillment, (2) filling the law with its deepest intended meaning, (3) embodying the law in His own life and imparting that righteousness to His people through union with Him. The antitheses demonstrate sense (2); the Gospel demonstrates sense (3). Carson argues the Sermon cannot be properly applied until the cross and resurrection have been understood as the ground of the new heart that obeys it.

- **John Stott** (*The Message of the Sermon on the Mount*, 1978): On the Beatitudes — Stott observes that the eight beatitudes describe a *single person* — Jesus Himself — who is uniquely poor in spirit (Phil 2:7), mourning (John 11:35), meek (Matt 11:29), hungry for righteousness (John 4:34), merciful (Luke 23:34), pure in heart (Heb 7:26), and peacemaking (Eph 2:14–15). The Beatitudes are first a Christological portrait before they are an ethical description. To become blessed is to become more like Christ.

- **Joachim Jeremias** (*The Sermon on the Mount*, 1961): Argues the Sermon is not law but gospel — not the condition for entering the Kingdom but the *description* of those who have entered it by grace. The indicative precedes every imperative: *"Yours is the Kingdom"* (v. 3) comes before any command. Jeremias' reading aligns with the Reformed indicative/imperative pattern: the Beatitudes are declarations of identity; the antitheses are the implications of that identity for conduct.

- **John Calvin** (*Commentary on Matthew*): On v. 48 — Calvin insists *teleios* does not refer to sinless perfection but to *integrity of heart* — undivided love for God expressed in love for neighbor, including enemy. He connects it to Deut 18:13 (*tāmîm* — blameless, wholehearted before God) and argues the command describes the *direction* of the Christian life, not a static achievement. Progressive sanctification is the movement toward this *telos.*

- **Herman Ridderbos** (*The Coming of the Kingdom*, 1962): Shows that the Sermon on the Mount is eschatological ethics — it describes the life of the Kingdom *already inaugurated* in Jesus' coming. The commands are not timeless ethical principles but the shape of life appropriate to those who live in the overlap of the ages — between the Kingdom's arrival and its consummation. This is why they require the Spirit: the power of the age to come is what enables Kingdom-age ethics in the present.

### ⚔️ Debate Corner — Three Contested Interpretations of the Sermon on the Mount

**Position 1: The Sermon as Impossible Ideal (Lutheran Law/Gospel)**
The Sermon's primary function is to drive the hearer to despair of self-righteousness and thereby to grace. The commands are intentionally impossible — the point of *"be ye therefore perfect"* is to show you cannot be, and so to throw you onto Christ. The Sermon is law in its convicting function, not a program for Christian living.

*Strength:* Rightly emphasizes the Sermon's crushing effect on self-reliance and the necessity of grace.
*Weakness:* Undersells the Sermon's role as guidance for *redeemed* believers. The disciples to whom Jesus speaks (v. 1) are already followers. The Sermon is addressed to the regenerate — not merely as mirror but as road map.

**Position 2: The Sermon as Kingdom Ethics for the Future Millennium (Dispensational)**
The Sermon describes the ethics of the coming millennial Kingdom — it is not directly applicable to the church age. The commands belong to a future dispensation when Christ reigns on earth.

*Weakness:* Matthew 5:1 addresses present disciples. The epistles repeatedly apply Sermon ethics to the present church (Rom 12:14–21 echoes Matt 5:38–44; Jas 5:12 echoes Matt 5:37). No NT author treats the Sermon as future-only material. The dispensational framework requires an interpretive layer the text itself does not supply.

**Position 3: The Sermon as the New Covenant Description of the Regenerate Life (Reformed)**
The Sermon describes the character and conduct of those who have entered the Kingdom through the new birth — enabled by the Spirit, grounded in grace, oriented toward the eschatological *telos* of v. 48. The Beatitudes are identity declarations (indicative); the antitheses are the implications (imperative). The command to *"be perfect"* is both a present trajectory and an eschatological destination. The Sermon is impossible without the Spirit — and the Spirit is given precisely to enable it (1 Thess 4:8; Ezek 36:27).

**Reformed assessment:** Position 3 is most exegetically consistent with the full canonical witness. It takes the commands seriously as present obligations, grounds them in the prior work of grace (the Beatitudes as identity), acknowledges their impossibility without the Spirit, and holds the eschatological horizon (v. 48; 1 John 3:2) as the destination the Spirit is moving believers toward. The Sermon is neither merely law (Position 1) nor deferred ethics (Position 2) — it is the Spirit-enabled life of the Kingdom community now.

⚠️ **Pastoral Warning:** The dispensational reading has produced churches that treat the Sermon as irrelevant to present Christian life — a serious pastoral failure. The Lutheran reading, taken to an extreme, produces believers who experience the Sermon only as guilt and never as direction. Both errors impoverish the church. Teach the Sermon as gift — the portrait of who you are becoming, made possible by the one who both sets the standard and gives the Spirit to meet it.

### 🔤 Original Language Deep Dive — *Plēroō* (Fulfil) in Matthew 5:17

**Word:** *πληρόω* — **plēroō**
**Parsing:** Aorist active infinitive — *plērōsai*
**Root:** *plēres* — full; *plēroō* — to fill, to make full, to bring to completeness
**Definition:** To fill to capacity; to bring to its intended fullness; to complete what was begun; to bring to its *telos*

**Semantic range in Matthew:**
- Prophetic fulfillment — Matt 1:22; 2:15,17,23; 4:14 — *"that it might be fulfilled which was spoken by the prophet"* — Jesus brings OT prophecy to its completion
- Completeness — Matt 13:48 — a net *"full"* (*plēroō*) of fish — filled to capacity
- Time fulfillment — Matt 23:32 — *"Fill ye up then the measure of your fathers"* — bring to its completion

**In Matt 5:17:** All three dimensions operate simultaneously:
1. *Prophetic* — Jesus fulfills what the OT anticipated about the coming lawgiver (Deut 18:15–18 — *"a prophet like unto me"*)
2. *Hermeneutical* — He fills the law with its deepest intent, revealing what it always meant
3. *Personal/eschatological* — He embodies the law perfectly in His own life (Heb 4:15) and then imparts that righteousness to His people (Rom 5:19; 8:4)

**The contrast with *kataluō* (destroy/demolish):**
*Kataluō* = to break down, dissolve, render ineffective — the word used for demolishing buildings (Matt 24:2 — *"there shall not be left here one stone upon another, that shall not be thrown down [kataluthēsetai]"*). Jesus is not demolishing one stone of the law. He is bringing every stone to its intended architectural fullness.

**LXX precedent:** *Plēroō* in the LXX often translates Hebrew *male'* — to fill, to complete, to fulfill a period of time or a purpose. The word carries the sense of something reaching its designed capacity — a vessel filled, a time completed, a promise kept. Jesus is the filling of the law's designed capacity — its *pleroma* (fullness), as Paul calls Him in Col 1:19 and 2:9.

### 💡 Advanced Hermeneutical Tip — The Beatitudes as Christological Portrait

The most powerful hermeneutical key to Matthew 5 that most teachers miss: **the Beatitudes describe Jesus before they describe the disciple.**

Work through them:
- *Poor in spirit* — Phil 2:7 — He emptied Himself
- *Mourn* — John 11:35; Isa 53:3 — a man of sorrows
- *Meek* — Matt 11:29 — *"I am meek and lowly in heart"*
- *Hunger and thirst after righteousness* — John 4:34 — *"My meat is to do the will of him that sent me"*
- *Merciful* — Luke 23:34 — *"Father, forgive them"*
- *Pure in heart* — Heb 7:26 — *"holy, harmless, undefiled"*
- *Peacemaker* — Eph 2:14–15 — *"He is our peace"*
- *Persecuted for righteousness* — 1 Pet 2:23; the entire Passion narrative

**Preaching application:** Before you apply any beatitude to your congregation, show them Jesus in it first. The beatitude is not primarily a command to *achieve* — it is a description of the one to whom they are being *conformed* (Rom 8:29 — *"predestinated to be conformed to the image of his Son"*). Sanctification is the Spirit pressing the image of the Beatitudes — the image of Christ — into the life of the believer. The Sermon is the portrait; the Spirit is the sculptor; the disciple is the clay.

### 🎯 Teaching Angle — Teaching Tier 2 + Top 3 Tough Questions

**Framework for Tier 2:** Use the **Iceberg + Lighthouse** double image:
- *Iceberg (vv. 21–48):* The antitheses expose the 90% underwater — the heart's anger, desire, integrity, and love orientation. *"Jesus doesn't police the tip — He goes to the mass below."*
- *Lighthouse (vv. 3–16):* The Beatitudes + Salt & Light describe what the transformed community looks like from the outside — visible, different, pointing to something beyond itself.
- *The horizon (v. 48):* The lighthouse is pointing toward *teleios* — the complete character that sees God face to face.

**Top 3 Tough Questions:**

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| *"Does 'be ye therefore perfect' mean we have to achieve sinless perfection?"* | No — *teleios* means *complete, whole, arrived at its telos.* The context (vv. 44–45) defines the perfection: love without discrimination, patterned on the Father. This is simultaneously the present ethical standard (love your enemies now) and the eschatological destination (fully complete at glorification — 1 John 3:2). Phil 3:12–15 shows Paul using *teleios* for both *mature believers now* and *the goal not yet fully reached* — holding both without contradiction. |
| *"Jesus says anger makes us liable to judgment (v. 22) — does that mean every moment of anger is sin?"* | The Greek *orgizomenos* is a present participle — describing one who *harbors* anger, not one who felt it momentarily. Eph 4:26 — *"Be ye angry, and sin not: let not the sun go down upon your wrath"* — affirms that anger itself is not sin; sustained, unresolved anger toward a person is. The distinction is between emotion (not sin) and cultivated resentment (sin). The antithesis targets the harbored root, not the passing feeling. |
| *"How can I love my enemies (v. 44) when I don't feel love for them?"* | *Agapate* (v. 44) is an *act* of will, not a *feeling* of affection. The fourfold command — bless, do good, pray, love — is entirely actionable regardless of feeling. You can pray for someone you feel nothing for. You can do good to someone you find difficult. Feelings of warmth may follow obedience; they are not required to precede it. The ground of the command (v. 45) — *"that ye may be the children of your Father"* — makes it an identity act: you act like your Father because you are His child, not because it feels natural yet. |
