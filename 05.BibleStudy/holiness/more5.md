# Three Entry Points Into Sanctification
## 1 Thessalonians 4:3 × Mark 7:21 × Matthew 5:3 (KJV)

---

## The Three Entry Points Side by Side

| Passage | Entry Point | First Word of Sanctification |
|---------|-------------|------------------------------|
| **1 Thess 4:3** | Paul — *Abstain from fornication* | **The body** — what you do with your flesh |
| **Mark 7:21** | Jesus — *From within, out of the heart* | **The heart** — where the body's behavior originates |
| **Matt 5:3** | Jesus — *Blessed are the poor in spirit* | **The spirit** — the posture that makes heart-change possible |

You've identified something precise: Paul starts at the **surface** (body), Jesus in Mark 7 goes to the **middle** (heart), and Jesus in Matthew 5 goes to the **deepest layer** (spirit). Each entry point assumes the one beneath it.

---

## Entry Point 1 — Paul: The Body (1 Thessalonians 4:3)

> *"For this is the will of God, even your sanctification, that ye should abstain from fornication."*

Paul's first word on sanctification is **behavioral and bodily** — *abstain from fornication.* Why does Paul start here?

Because he is writing to a **young, pressured church** in a Greco-Roman city saturated with sexual immorality. He is not writing a systematic theology — he is writing a pastoral letter. He starts where the battle is most visible and most immediate: the body.

But Paul is not being shallow. He knows the body is the **outermost ring** of a deeper problem. Notice how quickly he moves inward:
- v. 3 — *abstain from fornication* (the body's act)
- v. 4 — *possess his vessel in sanctification and honour* (the body governed by a sanctified will)
- v. 5 — *not in the lust of concupiscence* (the desire beneath the act)
- v. 7 — *God hath not called us unto uncleanness* (the calling that reorients the whole person)
- v. 8 — *who hath also given unto us his holy Spirit* (the Spirit who works from inside out)

Paul starts at the body — but within five verses he has arrived at the Spirit. He starts at the symptom and works toward the source. He is a wise pastor: he names the battle where his people are actually fighting it, then traces it to its root.

**What Paul's entry point tells us:** Sanctification has a **visible, testable, embodied dimension.** It is not only internal. A changed heart eventually shows up in a changed body. If there is no change in the body, the claimed inner transformation is suspect.

---

## Entry Point 2 — Jesus in Mark 7: The Heart

> *"For from within, out of the heart of men, proceed evil thoughts, adulteries, fornications, murders…"*

Look at what Jesus does with fornication: He puts it **second in line** — after *evil thoughts.* The body's act of fornication is not the starting point; it is the *output* of a prior heart condition. The heart thinks the evil thought first. Then the body acts.

This is Jesus going **one layer deeper than Paul's entry point.** Paul says *abstain from fornication* — address the body. Jesus says *fornication comes from the heart* — address the source.

Notice that Jesus lists *fornications* (*porneiai*) in the heart-list — the same word Paul uses in 1 Thessalonians 4:3 (*porneias*). This is not coincidence. **Paul and Jesus are talking about the same sin — but from different angles:**
- Jesus describes where it **comes from** (the heart)
- Paul commands what to **do about it** (abstain — and here is why: the Spirit has been given to govern the heart that produces it)

**The diagnostic implication:** If a believer is struggling with fornication — or any sexual sin — Paul says *stop.* Jesus says *yes, stop — but also ask: what is the heart producing this?* The body's battle is a symptom report. The real question is: what desire, what emptiness, what idol is the heart worshipping that is driving this output?

This is why external accountability alone never fully works. You can have someone checking your phone and still be feeding the heart's desire in a hundred other ways. Jesus' entry point demands not just behavioral management but **heart surgery.**

**What Jesus' Mark 7 entry point tells us:** Sanctification requires going **below the behavior** to the generative source. You cannot permanently change the output without changing the factory.

---

## Entry Point 3 — Jesus in Matthew 5: The Spirit (Poverty of Spirit)

> *"Blessed are the poor in spirit: for theirs is the kingdom of heaven."*

This is the **deepest entry point of all three** — and the most counterintuitive. Why does Jesus open the Sermon on the Mount — His inaugural Kingdom address — with *poverty of spirit?*

Because **poverty of spirit is the prerequisite for everything else.**

The Greek *ptōchos tō pneumati* — destitute in the spirit — describes a person who has come to the end of their own spiritual resources. Not merely humble. Not merely modest. **Bankrupt.** Empty-handed before God. No spiritual collateral. No inner reserves to draw on.

This is the entry point that makes the other two possible:

- **You cannot address the heart (Mark 7 entry point)** until you have stopped defending it. The Pharisees never got to heart-level sanctification because they were never poor in spirit — they believed their hearts were basically fine, just needing better rule-following. Poverty of spirit means: *my heart is exactly as bad as Jesus said it is in Mark 7, and I have nothing to offer in my defense.*

- **You cannot abstain from fornication by the power of the Spirit (1 Thess 4 entry point)** until you have stopped trusting your own willpower. Poverty of spirit is the posture that receives the Spirit's power — because it has abandoned confidence in self. It is the open hand that grace fills.

**The progression becomes clear:**

```
POOR IN SPIRIT (Matt 5:3)
        │
        │ — "I have nothing. I bring nothing."
        │ — This empties self-reliance
        ▼
HEART ACKNOWLEDGED (Mark 7:21)
        │
        │ — "The factory is broken — and I own it."
        │ — This locates the real problem
        ▼
BODY GOVERNED (1 Thess 4:3)
        │
        │ — "Now, by the Spirit, abstain."
        │ — This produces visible transformation
        ▼
SANCTIFICATION IN PROGRESS
```

**What Jesus' Matthew 5:3 entry point tells us:** Before you can deal with your heart (Mark 7), you must first **stop protecting it.** And before you can stop protecting it, you must arrive at poverty of spirit — the recognition that you have no spiritual wealth to negotiate with. You come to God empty. That emptiness is not a problem to be solved before sanctification begins — **it is the beginning of sanctification.**

---

## Why All Three Entry Points Are Necessary

Here is what happens when you have only one or two of the three:

| Missing Entry Point | What Goes Wrong |
|--------------------|-----------------|
| **No poverty of spirit (Matt 5:3)** | You try to clean up the heart by self-effort. You manage behavior without dying to self. Produces the disciplined Pharisee — externally impressive, internally unchanged. |
| **No heart diagnosis (Mark 7:21)** | You fight fornication at the surface without asking why the heart keeps producing it. You manage symptoms without treating the disease. Produces the exhausted moral failure — keeps falling in the same place, doesn't know why. |
| **No bodily application (1 Thess 4:3)** | You have great spiritual insight and deep heart awareness but no visible change in behavior. Produces the theologically sophisticated but morally undisciplined believer — knows all the right categories, but the body is still doing whatever it wants. |

**You need all three** — in the right order:

1. **Come empty** (Matt 5:3) — abandon self-sufficiency; open the hand
2. **Own the source** (Mark 7:21) — stop blaming the outside world; name the heart's output honestly
3. **Govern the body** (1 Thess 4:3) — by the Spirit, make the visible change; the inner work shows up in the outer life

## The Fornication Thread — A Specific Illustration

Since all three passages touch fornication specifically, trace it through all three entry points as one concrete example:

**Matt 5:3 applied to fornication:**
*"I am poor in spirit regarding sexual sin. I have no reserves of willpower or moral superiority. I come to God empty-handed, admitting I cannot fix this by myself. This poverty is the beginning — not the shame of failure, but the grace of starting from zero before God."*

**Mark 7:21 applied to fornication:**
*"Fornication comes from within — from my heart. Something in my heart is hungry, empty, seeking, or worshipping something other than God. The sexual behavior is not random — it is the output of a specific heart condition. What is my heart actually after? Comfort? Control? Significance? Intimacy? I need to name the idol beneath the sin, not just the sin itself."*

**1 Thess 4:3 applied to fornication:**
*"God's will — stated plainly — is that I abstain. Not manage. Not reduce. Abstain. The Spirit has been given to make this possible. My poverty of spirit opens me to the Spirit. My heart diagnosis shows me what to bring to the Spirit for healing. Now the Spirit produces what my willpower never could: actual, sustained abstinence — not because I'm strong, but because He is at work in me."*

## One Summary Statement

**Matthew 5:3 empties you of self so you can receive. Mark 7:21 shows you what needs to be brought to God. First Thessalonians 4:3 shows you what God's will looks like in the body when the Spirit has done His work — and fornication is the test case all three passages share, making the connection between them not accidental but architecturally precise.**
