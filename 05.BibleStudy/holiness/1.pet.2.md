## ✝️ CHRISTOLOGICAL ROOT
*The foundation before the study begins*

### 🔎 What Jesus Said/Did

Peter anchors the entire chapter in Christ — quoting three OT texts Jesus fulfilled:

> *"To whom coming, as unto a living stone, disallowed indeed of men, but chosen of God, and precious."* — 1 Pet 2:4 (KJV)

> *"Behold, I lay in Sion a chief corner stone, elect, precious: and he that believeth on him shall not be confounded."* — 1 Pet 2:6 (KJV) *(quoting Isa 28:16)*

> *"The stone which the builders disallowed, the same is made the head of the corner."* — 1 Pet 2:7 (KJV) *(quoting Ps 118:22)*

And Jesus' own words that 1 Peter 2 echoes throughout:

> *"Ye are the light of the world… Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and glorify your Father which is in heaven."* — Matthew 5:14, 16 (KJV)

> *"The Son of man came not to be ministered unto, but to minister, and to give his life a ransom for many."* — Matthew 20:28 (KJV)

> *"He that findeth his life shall lose it: and he that loseth his life for my sake shall find it."* — Matthew 10:39 (KJV)

### 🔗 The Bridge

First Peter 2 is architecturally built on Christ as the Living Stone (v. 4). Everything in the chapter flows from that foundation:
- The newborn's craving (vv. 1–3) is craving *Him* — the Lord is good (v. 3; Ps 34:8)
- The four identity declarations (v. 9) flow from union with *Him* — the Chosen Stone
- The pilgrim ethic (vv. 11–12) is *His* ethic — light shining, works glorifying the Father
- The submission to suffering (vv. 18–20) is *His* pattern — the suffering servant of Isaiah 53
- The explicit statement of v. 21 — *"Christ also suffered for us, leaving us an example, that ye should follow his steps"* — makes the Christological root of the whole chapter explicit

Without the Living Stone as foundation, the chapter's ethics become moralism. With Him as foundation, every command is a participation in His life and character.

### ⚠️ What Gets Lost Without This Root

Without Christ as the Living Stone foundation, 1 Peter 2 becomes a collection of social ethics — submit to government, behave well among pagans, servants obey masters — stripped of their gospel ground and reduced to civic virtue. With Christ as foundation, every submission and every suffering becomes a participation in the cross-shaped life of the Son of God.

🌍 **Missiological Note:** First Peter 2 is one of the most missiologically saturated chapters in the NT. The purpose clause of v. 9 (*"shew forth the praises"*), the witness motive of v. 12 (*"that… they may by your good works, which they shall behold, glorify God in the day of visitation"*), and the suffering-as-witness pattern of vv. 18–25 all converge on one point: **the holy life is the missionary life.** Peter's audience is diaspora — scattered among hostile Gentiles. Their holiness is not retreat from the world; it is their most powerful engagement with it.

## 🟢 TIER 1 — THE FOUNDATION
*New believers, youth, anyone starting from zero*

---

### 📌 Core Concept

Think of a house being built. Before the walls go up, before the roof goes on, before anyone moves in — someone lays the cornerstone. Everything else lines up from that one stone. If the cornerstone is right, the whole building is right. If it's wrong, everything else is crooked. First Peter 2 is about Jesus being that cornerstone — and about what happens to people who come to Him and are built into His house. They get a new identity (chosen, royal, holy, His). They get a new way of living (abstain, submit, follow His steps). And they get a new purpose (shew forth His praises to a watching world). The whole chapter is: *come to the Stone, become like the Stone, live for the glory of the one who laid the Stone.*

### 🪞 Analogy

Imagine a refugee who has been brought into a royal family — adopted, given the family name, enrolled in the household. On Day 1 they still feel like a refugee. But the King says: *"No — you are mine. Now live like it."* The refugee doesn't earn the name by behaving royally. The name is given first. The behavior is the response. That's 1 Peter 2. You were *"not a people"* (v. 10) — a spiritual refugee. God adopted you, named you chosen/royal/holy/peculiar, and now says: *"Live like who you are. Abstain. Submit. Follow My Son's steps. Shew forth My praises."* The identity comes first. The life follows.

## 🟡 TIER 2 — THE BUILDER
*Growing believers, small group leaders, Sunday school teachers*

---

### 📌 Deeper Dive

Here is the thing most readers miss about 1 Peter 2: it is not a chapter about ethics. It is a chapter about **architecture.** Peter is building something — and every section of the chapter is one more layer of the structure. He starts with the foundation material (newborn cravings — you need the Word to grow, v. 2). Then he introduces the cornerstone (Christ the Living Stone, v. 4). Then he tells you what the building is (a spiritual house of lively stones, v. 5). Then he declares what the building is called (chosen, royal, holy, peculiar — v. 9). Then he describes how the building relates to the surrounding neighborhood (pilgrim holiness, civic submission, household ethics — vv. 11–20). And then he shows you the architectural model for the whole structure (Christ's suffering — v. 21). Every section assumes every prior section. You cannot understand the ethics of vv. 11–20 without the identity of vv. 9–10. You cannot understand the identity of vv. 9–10 without the Stone of vv. 4–8. And you cannot come to the Stone without the newborn craving of vv. 1–3.

Peter is writing to diaspora Christians — scattered across five Roman provinces in Asia Minor (1:1) — who are experiencing social marginalization, verbal abuse, and the daily pressure of living as a minority in a pagan culture. He is not giving them a survival manual. He is giving them a **theology of identity in a hostile world.** The four declarations of v. 9 are not encouragement — they are armament. When the world tells you that you are nobody, Peter says: *"But ye are..."* When the world's voice says 'not a people,' God's voice says 'My people.' This is the pastoral architecture of 1 Peter 2.

The suffering section (vv. 18–25) is the most culturally confronting part of the chapter for modern readers. Peter is addressing *oiketai* — household servants, many of whom were slaves. He does not call for social revolution. He calls for something more radical: the cross-shaped response to unjust suffering. His argument is not *"this is just the way things are"* — it is *"this is what Jesus did, and you are called to follow His steps."* The cross is not just the ground of salvation — it is the pattern of the Christian life. This is perhaps the most demanding thing Peter says in the whole letter: the suffering of the innocent servant is not a problem to escape but a participation in the suffering of Christ (v. 21) that becomes a witness to the watching world (v. 12).

### 🔤 Original Language Insight — *Epipothēsate* (Desire/Crave) in v. 2

**Word:** *ἐπιποθήσατε* — **epipothēsate**
**Transliteration:** ep-ee-poth-AY-sat-eh
**Parsing:** Aorist active imperative, 2nd person plural — *"crave!"* — a decisive, urgent command
**Definition:** To long for intensely, to yearn after with strong desire — *epi* (upon/toward, intensifying) + *pothéō* (to yearn, to crave)

**The intensity of the word:**
*Epipothéō* is not the word for mild preference or casual interest. It describes the kind of craving that is urgent, urgent, and consuming. It is used of:
- Paul longing to see the Thessalonians (1 Thess 3:6) — a deep pastoral ache
- Paul's desire to see the Romans (Rom 1:11) — an urgent spiritual longing
- The Spirit's jealous yearning for the believer (Jas 4:5 — *"the spirit that dwelleth in us lusteth to envy"*)

**The newborn image:** *"As newborn babes"* (*hōs artigennēta brephē*) — just born. A newborn's cry for milk is not polite preference — it is urgent, immediate, consuming need. The comparison is precise: your relationship to the Word should have that quality of *need*, not *nice-to-have.*

**The object:** *"The sincere milk of the word"* — *to logikon adolon gala* — literally *"the rational/spiritual unadulterated milk."* *Logikon* connects to *logos* (Word) — the milk that is Word-shaped, Spirit-pure, undiluted. Not religious sentiment. Not inspirational thoughts. The *Word* — God's actual speech.

**Sanctification implication:** The craving for the Word is both the **evidence** of new birth and the **means** of growth. You did not produce the new birth — *"being born again… by the word of God"* (1 Pet 1:23). The same Word that created the life sustains and grows it. This connects directly to John 17:17 (*"Sanctify them through thy truth: thy word is truth"*) — the Word is the instrument of sanctification. Without the craving, there is no growth. Without the growth, the identity declarations of v. 9 remain positional without becoming experiential.

### ⚠️ Common Misconception

**Myth:** *"The submission passages in 1 Peter 2 (to government, vv. 13–17; to masters, vv. 18–20) mean Christians should passively accept all authority and never push back against injustice."*

**Truth:** Peter's submission commands are grounded in two things that limit them significantly. First, v. 13 — *"submit yourselves to every ordinance of man for the Lord's sake"* — the submission is *for the Lord's sake*, meaning it is an act of worship, not servility, and it is bounded by the Lord's authority (Acts 5:29 — *"we ought to obey God rather than men"*). Second, the pattern of Christ (vv. 21–23) — Jesus submitted to unjust suffering but *did not sin* in doing so. He did not validate the injustice; He endured it with integrity while *"committing himself to him that judgeth righteously"* (v. 23). The submission is not moral agreement with injustice — it is the strategic posture of someone who trusts God as the ultimate judge and uses the platform of unjust suffering as a witness (v. 12).

### 💡 Study Method — Structural Analysis

For a chapter as architecturally layered as 1 Peter 2, use **Structural Analysis** before diving into individual verses:

1. **Identify the foundation** — What is the theological foundation of the chapter? (Christ the Living Stone, vv. 4–8)
2. **Identify the identity section** — What does God declare about the readers before commanding anything? (vv. 9–10)
3. **Identify the imperative section** — Where do the commands begin, and what do they rest on? (v. 11 — *"therefore"* connects to vv. 9–10)
4. **Identify the pattern** — What is the model for the ethics? (Christ's suffering, vv. 21–25)
5. **Draw the architecture** — Sketch the chapter as a building: foundation (stone) → identity (name) → ethics (life) → model (Christ)

This structural reading prevents proof-texting and shows TONA members how the chapter's parts reinforce each other.

## 🔴 TIER 3 — THE MASTERCLASS
*Pastors, teachers, theology students, deep divers*

---

### 📌 Theological Framework

First Peter 2 spans seven major theological loci — making it one of the most theologically dense single chapters in the NT epistles:

**1. Sanctification — The Word as Means of Grace (vv. 1–3)**
The command to *"desire the sincere milk of the word"* (v. 2) establishes the Word as the primary instrument of sanctification — directly paralleling John 17:17 (*"sanctify them through thy truth"*) and Psalm 119:9 (*"Wherewithal shall a young man cleanse his way? by taking heed thereto according to thy word"*). The newborn craving is not optional enthusiasm — it is the organic expression of regeneration. Those born again *"by the word of God"* (1 Pet 1:23) naturally hunger for that Word. The absence of craving is a diagnostic — not necessarily of lostness, but of spiritual malnutrition or quenching of the Spirit.

**2. Christology — The Living Stone and the Two Responses (vv. 4–8)**
The Stone theology of vv. 4–8 weaves three OT texts — Isa 28:16 (chosen, precious cornerstone), Ps 118:22 (the rejected stone becoming the head of the corner), and Isa 8:14 (a stone of stumbling) — into a unified Christological statement. Christ is simultaneously *elect and precious* to believers (v. 7) and a *stone of stumbling* to disobedient unbelievers (v. 8). The same Stone divides all humanity into two responses. This is Peter's most compressed statement of the *krisis* (judgment/division) that Christ creates by His very presence — anticipating the final judgment without being identical to it.

**3. Ecclesiology — The Spiritual House and Corporate Priesthood (vv. 4–5, 9)**
The *"spiritual house"* (*oikos pneumatikos*) of v. 5 and the *"royal priesthood"* (*basileion hierateuma*) of v. 9 together constitute Peter's doctrine of the church. Individual believers are *lithoi zōntes* (living stones) being *oikodomeisthe* (passive — built up by another) into a corporate structure. Two implications: (a) the church is not a voluntary association of independent Christians — it is a divine construction project in which God is the builder; (b) every member has a priestly function — upward (intercession, spiritual sacrifices) and outward (witness, v. 9b). The Reformation's *priesthood of all believers* has its deepest NT root here.

**4. Covenant Theology — The New Covenant People (vv. 9–10)**
Peter's weaving of Exod 19:5–6 (Sinai covenant) and Hos 1:9–10; 2:23 (the broken covenant restored) into the four declarations of v. 9 is a masterclass in new covenant hermeneutics. The Sinai declaration, originally spoken over ethnic Israel conditionally (*"if ye will obey"* — Exod 19:5), is now spoken over the multiethnic church unconditionally — because Christ has fulfilled the condition. The Hosea restoration (*"not my people → my people"*) demonstrates that the expansion of the covenant to include Gentiles was always God's redemptive plan for covenant-breakers, now realized in Christ. Peter is not replacing Israel; he is showing the covenant's intended fullness, achieved in the Messiah.

**5. Ethics — The Pilgrim Community's Social Witness (vv. 11–17)**
The ethical section (vv. 11–17) is grounded in a *theology of witness.* The motive for holy living among the Gentiles (v. 12) is not social respectability but eschatological testimony: *"that they may by your good works… glorify God in the day of visitation."* The submission to governing authorities (vv. 13–17) is not political quietism — it is the strategic posture of a community that lives as *"free"* (v. 16) while using that freedom *"as the servants of God,"* not *"for a cloak of maliciousness."* The fourfold command of v. 17 — honor all, love the brotherhood, fear God, honor the king — establishes a hierarchy: all receive honor, the brotherhood receives love, God alone receives fear, the king receives honor but not fear.

**6. Suffering — The Cross-Shaped Life (vv. 18–20)**
The suffering section is Peter's most radical ethical claim: unjust suffering endured for conscience toward God is *charis* — grace (v. 20). Not merely acceptable or understandable — *grace.* This is because it participates in the pattern of Christ (v. 21). The cross is not merely the ground of salvation — it is the *model* of the Christian life. Unjust suffering, endured with integrity and without retaliation, becomes a participation in Christ's passion and a testimony to the watching world (v. 12). This is perhaps the most countercultural claim in the entire letter.

**7. Atonement and Sanctification — Isaiah 53 Applied (vv. 21–25)**
Peter's citation of Isaiah 53 in vv. 22–25 applies the Suffering Servant prophecy on two levels simultaneously: (a) *substitutionary atonement* — *"who his own self bare our sins in his own body on the tree"* (v. 24; Isa 53:4–5); (b) *moral example* — *"leaving us an example, that ye should follow his steps"* (v. 21). The two are not in tension — they are the two dimensions of the cross: what Christ did *for* us (objective atonement) and what Christ showed *to* us (moral pattern). Verse 24 contains perhaps the clearest single-verse statement of penal substitution in the NT: *"bare our sins… in his own body… that we, being dead to sins, should live unto righteousness."* The purpose of the substitution is explicit — not merely forgiveness but *"live unto righteousness."* Atonement is the ground of sanctification, not its substitute.

### 📚 Scholarly Insight

- **Wayne Grudem** (*1 Peter*, TNTC, 1988): On vv. 4–8 — Grudem traces the three Stone texts (Isa 28:16; Ps 118:22; Isa 8:14) and shows they were already being combined in Jewish interpretation before Peter. Jesus Himself had applied Ps 118:22 to Himself (Matt 21:42). Peter's contribution is the explicit trinitarian grounding: the Stone is *"chosen of God"* (election), *"precious"* (the Father's evaluation), and *"living"* (the resurrection, implying the Spirit). The Stone Christology is implicitly trinitarian.

- **Thomas Schreiner** (*1, 2 Peter, Jude*, NAC, 2003): On v. 8 (*"whereunto also they were appointed"*) — Schreiner argues that *etethēsan* (were appointed) refers to the appointment to stumble as a consequence of disobedience, not the appointment to disobedience itself. This preserves human responsibility while maintaining divine sovereignty. The text does not say they were appointed to disobey — it says those who disobey were appointed to stumble. The sequence is: disobedience → stumbling, and God has sovereignly ordained that this consequence follows.

- **Edmund Clowney** (*The Message of 1 Peter*, BST, 1988): On the *"spiritual house"* (v. 5) — Clowney argues the temple imagery is the key to understanding 1 Peter's entire ecclesiology. The church is not merely a community but a *dwelling place of God* — the new temple that replaces the Jerusalem temple destroyed in AD 70 (likely already destroyed or imminent when Peter writes). The *"spiritual sacrifices"* are not metaphors for the real thing — they *are* the real thing now that Christ has fulfilled and superseded the sacrificial system.

- **Karen Jobes** (*1 Peter*, BECNT, 2005): On the submission passages (vv. 13–20) — Jobes situates them in the *haustafeln* (household code) tradition common to NT ethics (Col 3:18–4:1; Eph 5:22–6:9; Titus 2:1–10) and argues that Peter's innovation is the Christological grounding (vv. 21–25). Other haustafeln give social or natural grounds for submission; Peter gives the cross. The suffering servant pattern makes the submission commands radically different from mere social conformity — they are a participation in Christ's own posture before the Father and before unjust authorities.

- **John Calvin** (*Commentaries on the Catholic Epistles*): On v. 24 — Calvin insists the phrase *"bare our sins in his own body on the tree"* is the clearest NT statement of penal substitution: Christ bore not the punishment resembling ours but the punishment *ours* — our actual guilt transferred to Him. And the purpose clause — *"that we, being dead to sins, should live unto righteousness"* — shows that the atonement was always designed to produce sanctification, not merely forgiveness. Forgiveness and transformation are not two separate gifts — they are two dimensions of the one gift of union with the crucified and risen Christ.

### ⚔️ Debate Corner — Three Contested Questions in 1 Peter 2

**1. Does v. 8 (*"whereunto also they were appointed"*) Teach Double Predestination?**

*Arminian view:* *Etethēsan* (were appointed) refers to the appointed *consequence* of disobedience, not the appointment to disobedience itself. God appointed that stumbling follows rejection — not that rejection was predetermined. Human responsibility is preserved.

*High Calvinist view:* The passive *etethēsan* reflects God's sovereign appointment of some to unbelief and its consequence — consistent with Rom 9:18–22 (God hardening whom He will) and Jude 4 (*"certain men… of old ordained to this condemnation"*).

*Reformed Confessional view:* The text teaches divine sovereignty over the consequence of disobedience without explicitly teaching reprobation (the predestination of individuals to damnation). The WCF (3.7) affirms reprobation but does not require this text to carry the full weight of that doctrine. The text establishes that stumbling is within God's sovereign purpose — the precise mechanism of how God relates to the individual's disobedience is addressed more fully in Romans 9.

**Reformed assessment:** The Reformed confessional view is most exegetically careful. The text clearly teaches divine sovereignty — the stumbling of unbelievers is not outside God's purpose. Whether this extends to the full doctrine of double predestination is better established from Romans 9 than from this text alone. Avoid over-reading *etethēsan* into a full doctrine of reprobation — but equally avoid evacuating it of its clear assertion of divine sovereignty.

**2. Does v. 21 Make Christ Primarily a Moral Example (Liberal/Socinian) or Primarily a Substitutionary Sacrifice?**

*Moral Influence view (Liberal):* The primary point of Christ's suffering is the example it sets — v. 21 (*"an example… follow his steps"*) is primary; v. 24 is secondary or figurative.

*Penal Substitution view (Reformed/Evangelical):* Verse 24 is the theological ground of v. 21 — the example is possible and meaningful only because the substitution has already occurred. *"Who his own self bare our sins in his own body on the tree"* is the clearest single-verse statement of penal substitution in the NT. The example rests on the atonement; the atonement is not reduced to an example.

**Reformed assessment:** The penal substitution view is exegetically and contextually decisive. The *hypogrammos* (writing pattern/example) of v. 21 is introduced by *"for even hereunto"* — connecting it to the suffering servants of vv. 18–20. But the explicit atonement language of vv. 22–24 (*"bare our sins… on the tree… by whose stripes ye were healed"* — all Isa 53 substitutionary language) makes the substitution the theological foundation. You can follow the example precisely *because* the substitution has freed you from sin's dominion (v. 24 — *"dead to sins, live unto righteousness"*).

**3. Are the Submission Commands (vv. 13–20) Culturally Relative or Permanently Binding?**

*Cultural relativism view:* The submission to masters (vv. 18–20) was addressed to a slave culture and is culturally conditioned — not applicable today except in analogous situations (employees, etc.).

*Principlist view (Reformed):* The submission commands are culturally expressed but principially permanent. The *principle* — the cross-shaped response to unjust authority, grounded in Christ's pattern (vv. 21–23) — is permanently binding. The *application* varies by cultural context: what slavery was in the first century, employer-employee relationships and other power structures are today. The principle of enduring unjust treatment with integrity, without retaliation, while trusting God as the ultimate judge (v. 23) — applies wherever unjust authority is exercised.

**Reformed assessment:** The principlist view is most consistent with the Christological grounding of vv. 21–25. Peter does not say *"submit to masters because slavery is right"* — he says *"submit because this is what Christ did, and you are called to follow His steps."* The cross-shaped ethic is permanent; its cultural expressions vary.

⚠️ **Pastoral Warning:** The submission passages have been historically abused to silence victims of genuine abuse and injustice. Peter's model is Christ — who endured unjust suffering *without sin* (v. 22). When *submission* requires *sin*, the command has reached its ceiling (Acts 5:29). Peter is not commanding victims to remain in abusive situations; he is calling believers to respond to the normal injustices of life with cross-shaped integrity rather than retaliation. Teach this distinction explicitly and pastorally.

### 🔤 Original Language Deep Dive — *Hypogrammos* (Example) in v. 21

**Word:** *ὑπογραμμός* — **hypogrammos**
**Parsing:** Noun, masculine, accusative singular
**Literal meaning:** *Under-writing* — a writing pattern placed beneath; the line of letters a student traces

**Etymology:** *hypo* (under) + *gramma* (letter/writing). In ancient education, the teacher would write the alphabet or a sentence at the top of a wax tablet, and the student would trace (*hypo-graphō*) the teacher's letters underneath. The *hypogrammos* was the master's writing; the student's task was to reproduce it faithfully below.

**Usage outside Scripture:** Found in Greek educational literature for writing exercises. Also used for architectural templates — the pattern a builder traces to cut stones correctly. Both usages are relevant: Peter's audience includes both the literate (who would recognize the schoolroom image) and those connected to building trades (who would recognize the architectural image — especially given the Stone metaphor of vv. 4–8).

**Theological weight in 1 Peter 2:21:**
The image is profound on multiple levels:
1. **The master writes first** — Christ suffered *before* you are called to follow. He does not hand you a blank tablet and say *"figure it out."* He writes the pattern first, in His own suffering.
2. **The student traces, not invents** — You do not create your own version of holiness or suffering. You trace what He has already written. This is the *imitatio Christi* — not autonomous self-development but faithful reproduction of the divine pattern.
3. **The pattern is below the writing** — *Hypo* (under) suggests the student's tracing is *beneath* the teacher's original. Christ's suffering infinitely exceeds and underlies ours. We trace what He wrote; we never equal or replace it.
4. **The writing is what makes tracing possible** — You can only follow His steps because He first *bore your sins* (v. 24). The atonement is the writing on the tablet. The imitation is the tracing beneath. Without the atonement, there is nothing to trace — only a blank tablet and an impossible demand.

**Sanctification implication:** The *hypogrammos* image holds together the two dimensions of the cross that the entire chapter unifies: Christ's suffering *for* us (the writing — vv. 22–24) and Christ's suffering *as pattern for* us (the tracing — v. 21). Sanctification is not self-improvement by Christ's example — it is Spirit-enabled tracing of the divine pattern that Christ's atonement made possible.

### 💡 Advanced Hermeneutical Tip — The Isaiah 53 Substructure

First Peter 2:21–25 is the most sustained NT application of Isaiah 53 outside of the Passion narratives themselves. Mapping the citations reveals the interpretive architecture:

| 1 Peter 2 | Isaiah 53 | Theological Function |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| v. 22 — *"Who did no sin, neither was guile found in his mouth"* | Isa 53:9 — *"he had done no violence, neither was any deceit in his mouth"* | Establishes the innocence of the sufferer — the substitution requires a sinless substitute |
| v. 23 — *"when he was reviled, reviled not again; when he suffered, he threatened not"* | Isa 53:7 — *"he was oppressed, and he was afflicted, yet he opened not his mouth"* | Establishes the pattern — the *hypogrammos* is traced from the Servant's silence |
| v. 24 — *"who his own self bare our sins in his own body on the tree"* | Isa 53:4,12 — *"he hath borne our griefs… he bare the sin of many"* | Establishes the substitution — the Servant bears what belongs to others |
| v. 24 — *"by whose stripes ye were healed"* | Isa 53:5 — *"with his stripes we are healed"* | Establishes the effect — healing flows from the Servant's suffering |
| v. 25 — *"ye were as sheep going astray"* | Isa 53:6 — *"all we like sheep have gone astray"* | Establishes the condition before — the wandering that required the shepherd |
| v. 25 — *"the Shepherd and Bishop of your souls"* | Isa 40:11 — *"He shall feed his flock like a shepherd"* (Isaiah's portrait of the divine shepherd) | Establishes the identity of the Servant — the Suffering Servant is the divine Shepherd |

**Preaching application:** Walk your congregation through this table. Show them that Peter is not prooftexting — he is doing sustained biblical theology. Isaiah 53 is the *score*; 1 Peter 2:21–25 is the *performance.* Every note Peter plays was written seven centuries earlier. And the final note — *"the Shepherd and Bishop of your souls"* (v. 25) — reveals what Isaiah glimpsed: the Suffering Servant and the divine Shepherd are one and the same Person. Jesus.

### 🎯 Teaching Angle — Teaching Tier 2 + Top 3 Tough Questions

**Framework:** Use the **Architect + Blueprint** image:
- *The Architect* (vv. 4–8): Christ the Living Stone is both the architect and the cornerstone — the design comes from Him; the building is defined by Him
- *The Blueprint* (vv. 9–10): The four identity declarations are the blueprint — what the building is named and intended to be
- *The Construction* (vv. 11–20): The daily work of building — abstaining, submitting, enduring — is the construction process
- *The Pattern* (vv. 21–25): The master's own *hypogrammos* — Christ's suffering is the template the construction follows

**Top 3 Tough Questions:**

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| *"Does 'bare our sins in his own body' (v. 24) mean Jesus literally became sinful?"* | No — *"Who did no sin"* (v. 22) makes His personal sinlessness explicit. *"Bare our sins"* (*anēnegken tas hamartias hēmōn*) means He bore the *consequence and weight* of our sins — the punishment they deserved — not that He *became* sinful. This is the priestly language of Lev 14:20 (bearing the offering). He bore what was ours without it becoming His own character. 2 Cor 5:21 — *"made him to be sin for us, who knew no sin"* — carries the same tension: the transaction is judicial, not ontological. |
| *"How can 'lively stones' (v. 5) teach that church community is essential — can't I be a Christian without being in a church?"* | The grammar answers the question: *"are built up"* (*oikodomeisthe*) is a plural passive — you are being built *with others*, by God as builder. You cannot be a lone stone in a spiritual house; a stone outside the house is just rubble. The NT knows no category of the lone believer detached from the corporate community — *"forsake not the assembling of yourselves together"* (Heb 10:25). The *"spiritual sacrifices"* (v. 5) are offered *together* as a priestly community. Solitary Christianity is a contradiction of Peter's ecclesiology. |
| *"Verse 13 says to submit to 'every ordinance of man' — does that include unjust laws?"* | The submission has a stated limit: it is *"for the Lord's sake"* (v. 13) — meaning the submission itself is an act of worship toward a higher authority. When obedience to human authority requires disobedience to God, the higher authority takes precedence — Acts 5:29 (*"We ought to obey God rather than men"*). Peter himself defied the Sanhedrin when required to stop preaching. The principle is: submit as far as God's authority permits; resist where God's authority requires it. The test is not preference but *conscience toward God* (v. 19). |

Isaiah saw them separately — chapter 40 (the divine Shepherd) and chapter 53 (the Suffering Servant). Peter brings them together in one Person. Preach that: the one whose stripes healed you is the one whose voice calls you home. The cross and the crook are held by the same hands."*
