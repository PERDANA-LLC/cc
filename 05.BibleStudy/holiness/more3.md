## First — A Quick Recap of What Each Passage Establishes

| Passage | Author | What It Does |
|---------|--------|--------------|
| **Matthew 5** | Jesus (Sermon on the Mount) | Redefines the *standard* of holiness — from external compliance to internal transformation |

| **1 Thessalonians 4** | Paul | Applies the *practice* of holiness — body, love, work, hope — in real Christian life |

These three are not parallel passages saying the same thing. They are **sequential** — each one assumes and builds on what came before.

## Matthew 5 — What Jesus Adds That Mark 7 Doesn't Cover

Mark 7 diagnoses the *problem* — the heart is the source of defilement. Matthew 5 goes further and defines the *standard* the heart must be transformed toward. They are companion passages, not competitors.

### The Six Antitheses (Matt 5:21–48) — The Standard Goes Deeper

Jesus uses the pattern *"Ye have heard… but I say unto you"* six times. Each time He does not abolish the law — He reveals that the law was always targeting the *heart*, and the Pharisees had settled for the surface.

| Old Hearing | Jesus Says | Heart Issue Exposed |
|-------------|-----------|---------------------|
| *"Thou shalt not kill"* (v. 21) | Anger and contempt are already murder (v. 22) | The heart's violence, not just the hand's act |
| *"Thou shalt not commit adultery"* (v. 27) | Looking with lust is already adultery (v. 28) | The heart's desire, not just the body's act |
| *"Whosoever shall put away his wife"* (v. 31) | Divorce for any cause hardens and harms (v. 32) | The heart's covenant faithfulness |
| *"Thou shalt not forswear thyself"* (v. 33) | Let your yes be yes — no oath-taking needed (v. 37) | The heart's integrity |
| *"An eye for an eye"* (v. 38) | Turn the other cheek (v. 39) | The heart's response to injustice |
| *"Thou shalt love thy neighbour"* (v. 43) | Love your enemies (v. 44) | The heart's capacity for grace beyond what's deserved |

**The cumulative point:** The law was always about the heart. The Pharisees had reduced it to behavior management. Jesus restores the law's full depth — and in doing so, reveals that no one can meet it on their own. This is the setup for the entire gospel.

## Now — The Three-Way Correlation

### 1. On the Heart

| Matthew 5 | Mark 7 | 1 Thessalonians 4 |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| *"Blessed are the pure in heart: for they shall see God."* (v. 8) | *"For from within, out of the heart of men, proceed evil thoughts, adulteries, fornications, murders…"* (vv. 21–22) | *"For God hath not called us unto uncleanness, but unto holiness."* (v. 7) |
| The pure heart is the *goal* | The corrupt heart is the *diagnosis* | Holiness is the *calling* that replaces corruption |
| **Standard set** | **Problem named** | **Direction given** |

**The arc:** Matt 5 says *this is where God's standard lands — purity of heart.* Mark 7 says *this is what the heart actually produces on its own — the vice list.* 1 Thess 4 says *this is what God does about it — He calls you out of that and into holiness by His Spirit.*

Together they form a complete doctrine of the heart:
- The **benchmark** (Matt 5:8 — pure heart sees God)
- The **baseline** (Mark 7:21–23 — the heart left to itself)
- The **bridge** (1 Thess 4:7–8 — the Spirit given to produce what the law demanded)

---

### 2. On Sexual Purity

| Matthew 5 | Mark 7 | 1 Thessalonians 4 |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| *"Whosoever looketh on a woman to lust after her hath committed adultery with her already in his heart."* (v. 28) | *"adulteries, fornications… lasciviousness"* listed as heart outputs (vv. 21–22) | *"that ye should abstain from fornication… that every one of you should know how to possess his vessel in sanctification and honour."* (vv. 3–4) |
| Lust begins in the *heart* | Sexual sin is produced *by* the heart | The body must be governed *through* sanctification |
| **Where it starts** | **What it produces** | **How to govern it** |

**The arc:** Jesus in Matthew 5 says sexual sin begins before the body acts — it begins in the heart's gaze and desire. Jesus in Mark 7 confirms sexual sin is a *heart output* — adulteries, fornications, lasciviousness come from within. Paul in 1 Thessalonians 4 then gives the practical answer: the Spirit-enabled governance of the body (*"possess his vessel in sanctification"*) is the only way to address what Jesus located in the heart.

You cannot address 1 Thessalonians 4:3–5 honestly without understanding Matthew 5:27–28 and Mark 7:21. Paul is not giving a new command — he is giving the practical outworking of what Jesus already diagnosed.

---

### 3. On the Law — Fulfillment, Not Abolition

| Matthew 5 | Mark 7 | 1 Thessalonians 4 |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| *"Think not that I am come to destroy the law, or the prophets: I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil."* (v. 17) | *"Making the word of God of none effect through your tradition."* (v. 13) | *"He therefore that despiseth, despiseth not man, but God, who hath also given unto us his holy Spirit."* (v. 8) |
| Jesus *fulfils* the law's full intent | The Pharisees *nullified* God's Word with tradition | Rejecting holiness is rejecting the God who commanded it |
| **Law's purpose restored** | **Law's corruption exposed** | **Law's authority grounded in the Giver** |

**The arc:** Matthew 5 establishes that Jesus came to *fulfill* — to reveal the law's deepest intent, not discard it. Mark 7 exposes the opposite move — human tradition that *nullifies* what God said. 1 Thessalonians 4 then shows what fulfillment looks like in actual life: holiness is not man's standard but God's — and to reject it is to reject the Spirit He gave.

The three passages together demolish two errors simultaneously:
- **Legalism** — following rules while the heart is unchanged (Mark 7's Pharisees)
- **Antinomianism** — treating Jesus' fulfillment as the law's abolition (misreading Matt 5:17 and Mark 7:19)

---

### 4. On Anger, Murder, and the Vice List

| Matthew 5 | Mark 7 | 1 Thessalonians 4 |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| *"Whosoever is angry with his brother without a cause shall be in danger of the judgment."* (v. 22) | *"murders… wickedness… pride… foolishness"* (vv. 21–22) | *"That no man go beyond and defraud his brother in any matter: because that the Lord is the avenger of all such."* (v. 6) |
| Anger in the heart = murder's root | Murder on the list of heart outputs | Defrauding a brother = injustice God will judge |

**The arc:** Matthew 5 establishes that relational sin — contempt, anger, dismissiveness — is murder at the root. Mark 7 lists *murders* and *wickedness* as natural heart outputs. Paul in 1 Thessalonians 4 applies this to the most subtle form — economic and relational exploitation of a brother. The standard of the Sermon (love your enemy, v. 44) becomes the law of the community Paul is protecting.

---

### 5. On Love Beyond the Minimum

| Matthew 5 | Mark 7 | 1 Thessalonians 4 |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| *"Love your enemies, bless them that curse you."* (v. 44) | *"For this saying go thy way; the devil is gone out of thy daughter."* (v. 29) — Jesus crosses ethnic lines to bless a Gentile enemy-adjacent woman | *"And indeed ye do it toward all the brethren which are in all Macedonia: but we beseech you, brethren, that ye abound more and more."* (v. 10) |
| Love without borders — the standard | Love without borders — demonstrated | Love without borders — commanded to increase |
| **Commanded** | **Embodied** | **Applied** |

**The arc:** Jesus commands love beyond tribe in Matthew 5. He then *demonstrates* it in Mark 7 by healing the Syrophoenician woman's daughter — love crossing every ethnic and religious boundary. Paul in 1 Thessalonians 4 calls the church to the same love: brotherly love (*philadelphia*) that extends across the whole of Macedonia, not just their local group. Command → Demonstration → Application.

---

### 6. On Wholeness — Seeing God, Being Opened, Complete Sanctification

| Matthew 5 | Mark 7 | 1 Thessalonians 4 |
|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| *"Blessed are the pure in heart: for they shall see God."* (v. 8) | *"Ephphatha — Be opened."* (v. 34) | *"And the very God of peace sanctify you wholly; and I pray God your whole spirit and soul and body be preserved blameless unto the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ."* (1 Thess 5:23 — the chapter's conclusion) |
| Purity of heart → seeing God | Deaf ears opened → hearing and speaking restored | Whole person sanctified → ready for the Return |
| **The destination** | **The miracle** | **The prayer** |

**The arc:** The Beatitude promises that the pure in heart *will see God* — that is the eschatological destination of sanctification. Jesus in Mark 7 *opens* what is closed — physical ears as a sign of what He does spiritually. Paul's great prayer at the close of 1 Thessalonians is that God would sanctify the whole person — spirit, soul, and body — *completely*, so they are ready to *see* the Lord at His return. The Beatitude's promise, the miracle's sign, and the apostle's prayer are all pointing at the same moment: the face-to-face encounter with Christ that holiness is preparing us for.

## One-Sentence Summary of the Entire Arc

**Matthew 5 tells you how deep the standard goes; Mark 7 tells you how broken the source is; 1 Thessalonians 4 tells you what God does about it — and all three agree: holiness is an inside-out work that begins in the heart, is produced by the Spirit, and is completed when we see Christ face to face.**

## Teaching Application for TONA

If you ever teach **holiness or sanctification** to your group, this is your three-text backbone:

1. **Open with Matthew 5** — show them the standard is deeper than they thought (heart, not just behavior)
2. **Move to Mark 7** — show them the heart is more broken than they thought (the factory is the problem)
3. **Land on 1 Thessalonians 4** — show them God is more committed than they thought (He calls, gives the Spirit, and is coming back to complete the work)

That sequence is the full gospel arc on holiness — and it's directly from Jesus and His apostle.
