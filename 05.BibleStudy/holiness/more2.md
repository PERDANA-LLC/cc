## 🔗 Mark 7:14–23 Correlated with the Sanctification Framework

---

### What Jesus Actually Establishes in Mark 7

> *"There is nothing from without a man, that entering into him can defile him: but the things which come out of him, those are they that defile the man."* — Mark 7:15 (KJV)

> *"For from within, out of the heart of men, proceed evil thoughts, adulteries, fornications, murders, thefts, covetousness, wickedness, deceit, lasciviousness, an evil eye, blasphemy, pride, foolishness: All these evil things come from within, and defile the man."* — Mark 7:21–23 (KJV)

Jesus makes two explosive moves here:

**Move 1 — He relocates the source of defilement.**
The Pharisees had built an entire external holiness system — ritual washing, food laws, purity codes — on the assumption that defilement came *from outside* a person. Jesus flips it entirely: the problem is the *heart*. External religion cannot produce holiness because external religion cannot fix the heart.

**Move 2 — He implicitly declares all foods clean (Mark 7:19).**
Mark's editorial note — *"purging all meats"* — signals that Jesus is not merely commenting on handwashing traditions. He is dismantling the entire category of *external* sources of defilement. If food cannot defile, then the entire external purity system has been superseded — because what it was pointing to has arrived.

| Defilement comes from the *heart* (v. 21) | Sanctification targets the *inner man* — spirit, soul, and body (1 Thess 5:23) | Both locate the real problem and real solution in the interior life |

### The Key Theological Payoff

Mark 7 answers a question the sanctification study left implicit: **Why can't external religion produce holiness?**

Jesus' answer: *because the factory producing sin is the heart itself.* You can scrub the outside of the vessel all day — but if the source of contamination is internal, external scrubbing is useless.

This is why **sanctification must be a work of the Spirit from the inside out** — which is exactly what Ezekiel 36:26–27 promised, what the new covenant delivers, and what Paul unpacks in Romans 8 and 2 Corinthians 3:18.

### And 1 Timothy 4:4–5 Ties It Together on Food

> *"For every creature of God is good, and nothing to be refused, if it be received with thanksgiving: For it is sanctified by the word of God and prayer."* — 1 Tim 4:4–5 (KJV)

Paul uses the word *hagiazetai* — **sanctified** — for food. The logic: what God created is good; what is received in faith and prayer is set apart (*sanctified*) for holy use. This is Mark 7:19 turned into doctrine. Jesus cleared the ground; Paul built the framework on it.

The deeper principle Paul is establishing — which connects back to the full sanctification study — is that **sanctification is not about the object, it's about the relationship.** Food isn't holy because of what it is; it's sanctified because of *who receives it and how*. The same is true of the believer: you're not holy because of your performance, but because of your union with the Holy One who receives you.

### One Sentence Summary of the Correlation

**Jesus (Mark 7) diagnosed that holiness is an inside-out problem; the new covenant (Ezek 36) promised the inside-out solution; Paul (Romans 8, 1 Thess 4–5, 1 Tim 4) explained how the Spirit delivers it — and the food question is the concrete test case that runs through all three.**
