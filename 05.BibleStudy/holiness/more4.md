## ✝️ CHRISTOLOGICAL ROOT
*The foundation before the study begins*

### 🔎 What Jesus Said/Did

> *"Think not that I am come to destroy the law, or the prophets: I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil."* — Matthew 5:17 (KJV)

> *"Blessed are the pure in heart: for they shall see God."* — Matthew 5:8 (KJV)

> *"Whosoever looketh on a woman to lust after her hath committed adultery with her already in his heart."* — Matthew 5:28 (KJV)

> *"There is nothing from without a man, that entering into him can defile him: but the things which come out of him, those are they that defile the man."* — Mark 7:15 (KJV)

> *"For from within, out of the heart of men, proceed evil thoughts, adulteries, fornications, murders…"* — Mark 7:21 (KJV)

### 🔗 The Bridge

In Matthew 5, Jesus sets the standard so high that no external religion can reach it — *"pure in heart"* is the only pass for seeing God, and lust in the heart is already adultery. In Mark 7, He explains why that standard is so devastating: the heart is not a neutral instrument that needs better instructions — it is the *factory* of every sin on the list. Together, these two passages create a theological crisis that Paul resolves in 1 Thessalonians 4: the God who sets an impossible standard and diagnoses an irreparably broken source has also *called* us unto holiness, *given* us His Spirit, and is *coming back* to complete what He started. The three passages are not independent — they are one argument in three acts.

*New believers, youth, anyone starting from zero*

### 📌 Core Concept

Imagine you're trying to clean a swimming pool. You can skim the leaves off the surface every day — but if the pipe pumping water into the pool is broken and full of mud, the pool will never be truly clean no matter how much you skim. That's the problem Jesus is describing across these three passages. In Matthew 5 He says the standard is *pure water* — all the way through. In Mark 7 He says the pipe itself is the problem — the heart is pumping out the mud. And in 1 Thessalonians 4, Paul tells us the good news: God doesn't just hand you a better skimmer — He replaces the pipe. He calls you to holiness, gives you His Spirit, and is coming back to finish the job completely.

### 🪞 Analogy

Think of a GPS. The old religious system — the Pharisees' version — was like a GPS that only told you *what road to stay on* but gave you no help when the engine of your car was broken. Jesus in Matthew 5 and Mark 7 is saying: the engine is the problem, not the road. You need more than directions — you need a new engine. First Thessalonians 4 is Paul saying: *"God gave you a new engine — the Holy Spirit. Now drive."*

## 🟡 TIER 2 — THE BUILDER
*Growing believers, small group leaders, Sunday school teachers*

---

### 📌 Deeper Dive

Here's what makes this three-way correlation so explosive for teaching: most Christians know these passages individually but have never seen how they lock together as a *single argument*. When you see the arc, everything changes — you stop treating holiness as a behavior project and start seeing it as a theological reality that God is building from the inside out.

Matthew 5 is Jesus' inaugural address — the Sermon on the Mount opens His public ministry. He is not softening the law; He is revealing its full depth. The six antitheses (*"Ye have heard… but I say unto you"*) are not Jesus replacing Moses — they are Jesus reading Moses correctly for the first time in a generation. The Pharisees had built a version of Torah observance that could be managed externally. Jesus destroys that comfort in six moves, ending with *"Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect"* (Matt 5:48). That is not a challenge to try harder — it is a declaration that the standard exceeds what human effort can reach. It is the theological setup for everything the New Testament says about grace, Spirit, and transformation.

Mark 7 is Jesus providing the *diagnostic* that explains why the Pharisees' external religion had to fail. If the source of defilement is the heart, then washing your hands — or following every dietary law perfectly — addresses the wrong location entirely. The Pharisees were treating symptoms while the disease was deeper. And Jesus' list in vv. 21–22 is not hyperbole — it is a clinical inventory of what the unrenewed heart actually produces when opportunity arises. Any honest person reading that list finds themselves somewhere on it.

First Thessalonians 4 is Paul writing to a young church — weeks or months old — that is already under pressure from the Greco-Roman sexual culture surrounding them. He does not give them a motivational speech. He grounds his commands in three theological realities: God's *calling* (v. 7), God's *Spirit* (v. 8), and God's *return* (vv. 13–18). Every command in the chapter is bracketed by these three pillars. This is the pastoral application of what Jesus diagnosed — and it only makes sense if you understand that Paul knows the commands are impossible without the Spirit Jesus promised.

### 🔤 Original Language Insight — *Katharos* (Pure) vs. *Koinos* (Defiled)

Two Greek words anchor the entire three-passage arc — one from Matthew 5, one from Mark 7:

**Matthew 5:8 — *καθαρός* (katharos)** — pure, clean, uncontaminated
- Used of clean vessels (Matt 23:26), pure gold (Rev 21:18), cleansed lepers (Matt 8:3)
- In Matt 5:8, *katharos tē kardia* — pure *in* the heart — the inner life must be uncontaminated, not just the outer performance
- The LXX uses *katharos* for the ritual purity of the tabernacle — Jesus transfers this category from temple objects to the human heart

**Mark 7:2 — *κοινός* (koinos)** — common, defiled, ceremonially impure
- The opposite of *katharos* — what is *koinos* cannot approach the holy
- Jesus' radical move: He declares that external *koinosis* (contact-defilement) is not the real category; the heart itself is already *koinos* from within

**The word-pair reveals the entire arc:**
The Pharisees were obsessing over making things *katharos* by avoiding *koinos* contact. Jesus exposes that the heart itself is the source of *koinosis* — and the only path to genuine *katharotes* (purity) is the inside-out work the new covenant promises.

Paul's word in 1 Thessalonians 4:7 — *"uncleanness"* (*akatharsia*, literally *un-katharos*) — completes the circle: God has not called you to *akatharsia* (impurity) but to *hagiasmos* (sanctification). The same vocabulary moves from Jesus' beatitude → Jesus' diagnosis → Paul's command.

---

### ⚠️ Common Misconception

**Myth:** *"Matthew 5 raises the standard, Mark 7 lowers external requirements, so the net result is that the New Testament is actually more relaxed about holiness than the Old Testament."*

**Truth:** The exact opposite. Jesus raises the standard from *behavior* to *heart* — from external compliance to internal purity. That is a far harder standard, not a softer one. And Mark 7's point is not that less is required — it is that the *location* of the requirement has been correctly identified: the heart. When Paul then commands holiness in 1 Thessalonians 4, he is applying this impossibly high, correctly-located standard to every area of life — body, love, work, and hope. The New Testament vision of holiness is more demanding than the Pharisees', not less.

### 💡 Study Method — Thematic Tracing (Cross-Book Topical Study)

This three-passage correlation is itself a model of **Thematic Tracing** — one of the most powerful advanced study methods:

1. **Identify a theme** (here: holiness / the heart)
2. **Find the theme's root** — where does Jesus establish it? (Matt 5, Mark 7)
3. **Find the theme's application** — where do the apostles apply it? (1 Thess 4)
4. **Find the theme's completion** — where does it end eschatologically? (1 Thess 5:23; 1 John 3:2)
5. **Trace the vocabulary** — *katharos, koinos, hagiasmos, akatharsia* — across the passages

For TONA group facilitation: assign each person one passage, have them identify the heart theme, then bring all three together in discussion. The group builds the arc collaboratively.

## 🔴 TIER 3 — THE MASTERCLASS
*Pastors, teachers, theology students, deep divers*

---

### 📌 Theological Framework

This three-passage correlation sits at the convergence of five theological loci:

**1. Hamartiology** — Mark 7:21–23 is the most concentrated single-text statement of sin's *origin* in the Synoptics. The heart (*kardia*) as the generative source of every category of sin — dispositional, relational, sexual, violent — maps precisely onto Paul's hamartiology in Romans 1:21–32 and directly onto the Genesis 6:5 diagnosis (*"every imagination of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually"*). What Mark 7 establishes as Christ's authoritative teaching, Paul systematizes as the theological foundation of his anthropology.

**2. Law and Gospel** — Matthew 5:17–48 is the definitive NT text on the law's *telos* (purpose and goal). Jesus is not abolishing the law — He is revealing its intended depth, which the Pharisees had systematically reduced. The six antitheses demonstrate that every Mosaic command was targeting the heart's orientation, not merely the body's compliance. This is the fulfillment trajectory: the law pointed toward a heart-level righteousness that only the new covenant can produce (Jer 31:33 — *"I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts"*).

**3. Pneumatology** — The resolution of the Matthew 5 crisis (impossibly high standard) and the Mark 7 diagnosis (irreparably corrupt source) is the gift of the Spirit. First Thessalonians 4:8 — *"who hath also given unto us his holy Spirit"* — is the pneumatological hinge of the entire arc. The Spirit is not an optional enhancement; He is the only explanation for why Paul can issue commands that the heart of Mark 7 could never fulfill. Ezekiel 36:27 is the OT promise being fulfilled: *"I will put my spirit within you, and cause you to walk in my statutes."*

**4. Eschatology** — First Thessalonians 4 is the only passage in this trio that explicitly grounds holiness in the *parousia* (return of Christ). The resurrection hope (vv. 13–18) is not a separate topic appended to the holiness commands — it is the *eschatological motivation* for the entire chapter. Matthew 5:8 points to the same horizon: *"they shall see God"* — the beatitude is eschatological, not merely ethical. Holiness is the preparation for the face-to-face encounter. The arc ends not in behavior but in glory.

**5. New Covenant Theology** — The three-passage arc *is* the new covenant in microcosm. Jeremiah 31:31–34 promised: law written on the heart, God as their God, sins forgiven. Matthew 5 reveals the law's true depth (written on the heart is far more demanding than carved in stone). Mark 7 exposes why the old covenant's external system could not achieve it (the heart is the problem, not the menu). First Thessalonians 4 describes the new covenant in operation: the Spirit given, the commands possible, the completion guaranteed.

| Matt 5:17 | *"I am not come to destroy, but to fulfil."* | *"Fulfil"* — *plēroō* — not merely to obey but to fill to the full, to complete, to bring to its intended fullness. | Mark 7:21 | *"For from within, out of the heart of men, proceed evil thoughts…"* | *"Proceed"* — *ekporeuontai* — present tense: they continuously proceed. The twelve vices form a taxonomy covering thought (*dialogismoi*), sexuality (*porneiai, moicheiai*), violence (*phonoi*), acquisition (*klopai, pleonexia*), relational corruption (*ponēria, dolos, aselgeia*), and attitude (*blasphēmia, hyperēphania, aphrosynē*). |

### 📚 Scholarly Insight

- **D.A. Carson** (*The Sermon on the Mount*, 1978): On Matt 5:17–20, Carson argues that *plēroō* (fulfil) operates on three levels simultaneously — Jesus fulfills OT prophecy, fulfills the law's deepest intent, and fulfills the law in the sense of bringing it to its eschatological goal. The antitheses (vv. 21–48) are the practical demonstration of the third level — what the law was always *aiming at.*

- **John Murray** (*Principles of Conduct*, 1957): Argues that Matthew 5 demonstrates the law's *third use* (the law as guide for the redeemed) at its most demanding form — not external compliance but the transformation of desire, intention, and disposition. Murray shows that this is not a new standard but the original standard newly revealed by the one who gave the law.

- **Joel Marcus** (*Anchor Yale Bible: Mark 1–8*): On Mark 7:21–23, Marcus traces the twelve-vice list against Jewish apocalyptic literature (1QS, Testament of the Twelve Patriarchs) and demonstrates that Jesus' list is deliberately comprehensive — covering every domain of human life. This is not a catalogue of the worst sins; it is an anthropological statement about the heart's *universal* corruption.

- **F.F. Bruce** (*Word Biblical Commentary: 1 & 2 Thessalonians*): On 1 Thess 4:7–8, Bruce notes that Paul's three-pillar structure (calling, Spirit, return) is the pastoral architecture that prevents both legalism and antinomianism — the commands are grounded in grace (calling), enabled by grace (Spirit), and oriented toward grace (return). Remove any pillar and the holiness ethic collapses into either effort-based exhaustion or license.

- **Herman Bavinck** (*Reformed Dogmatics*, Vol. 3): Provides the systematic framework unifying all three passages — the law's function (Matt 5) is to reveal the standard; sin's nature (Mark 7) is to show why the standard cannot be met externally; the Spirit's work (1 Thess 4) is to produce internally what the law demanded externally. This is Bavinck's theology of the *ordo salutis* applied to the holiness arc.

### ⚔️ Debate Corner — The Relationship Between Matthew 5's Standard and 1 Thessalonians 4's Commands

**Position 1: Perfectionism (Wesleyan/Holiness tradition)**
Matthew 5:48 (*"be ye perfect"*) and 1 Thess 5:23 (*"sanctify you wholly"*) together indicate that complete eradication of the sinful nature is possible in this life through a crisis experience of entire sanctification. Mark 7's heart-list describes the *unregenerate* heart only — the fully sanctified believer is freed from this.

*Reformed response:* 1 John 1:8 directly contradicts sinless perfection in this life. *Teleios* (Matt 5:48) refers to mature completeness of character, not sinless perfection — confirmed by Phil 3:12 where Paul explicitly denies having *already* attained. The Mark 7 vice-list describes the heart's *ongoing temptations* even in the regenerate, not merely the unregenerate state — which is why Paul's mortification commands (Rom 8:13; Col 3:5) are addressed to believers.

**Position 2: Antinomianism (various)**
Mark 7:19 (all foods clean) + Matt 5:17 (fulfil, not destroy) = the ceremonial and moral law both superseded in Christ. The Christian is free from moral demands; the Spirit produces whatever holiness exists without human participation.

*Reformed response:* Romans 6:1–2 and 8:13 together destroy this reading. The Spirit's work is not passive production but active *mortification* — *"through the Spirit do mortify"* requires the believer's participation. Matthew 5's commands are intensified, not relaxed. Paul's *imperatives* in 1 Thess 4 are real commands requiring real obedience, not descriptions of what the Spirit automatically produces without human agency.

**Position 3: Reformed Progressive Sanctification**
Matthew 5 reveals the standard (heart-level righteousness); Mark 7 diagnoses why it cannot be self-achieved (the corrupt heart); 1 Thessalonians 4 describes the Spirit-enabled, human-participatory, eschatologically-oriented progressive movement toward that standard — which is finally completed only at glorification. The indicative grounds the imperative; the Spirit enables the effort; the *parousia* completes the work.

*Reformed assessment:* This is the most exegetically consistent position across all three passages. It takes the standard seriously (Matt 5), the diagnosis honestly (Mark 7), the commands really (1 Thess 4), and the eschatological completion hopefully (1 Thess 5:23; 1 John 3:2).

⚠️ **Pastoral Warning:** The perfectionism error has produced burned-out, shame-ridden believers who can't reconcile their ongoing sin with their supposed "entire sanctification." The antinomian error has produced morally careless Christians who use grace as a ceiling rather than a floor. Both errors are addressed by holding all three passages together — the standard, the diagnosis, and the Spirit-enabled practice.

### 🔤 Original Language Deep Dive — *Teleios* (Perfect/Complete) in Matthew 5:48

**Word:** *τέλειος* — **teleios**
**Root:** *telos* — end, goal, completion, purpose
**Definition:** Having reached its *telos* — complete, whole, mature, fully developed toward its intended purpose

**Parsing in Matt 5:48:** Adjective, nominative, masculine — predicate: *"be ye perfect [teleios]"*

**Semantic range:**
- *Mature* — 1 Cor 2:6; 14:20; Heb 5:14 — *"them that are of full age"* (KJV) — *teleioi*, mature believers
- *Complete/whole* — James 1:4 — *"that ye may be perfect [teleios] and entire, wanting nothing"*
- *Eschatologically complete* — Phil 3:12 — Paul denies being *already* *teleiōmenos* (perfected); v. 15 — *"let us therefore, as many as be perfect [teleios]"* — the same word used for present maturity, not sinless perfection

**In Matt 5:48:** The command *"be ye therefore perfect"* follows the command to love enemies (v. 44) and the statement that God sends rain on just and unjust alike (v. 45). The *teleios* here is explicitly modeled on the Father's completeness of *love* — love that extends without discrimination. Jesus is not commanding sinless perfection; He is commanding a completeness of love that reflects the Father's character. This is simultaneously an ethical standard *and* an eschatological goal — the *telos* we are moving toward (1 Thess 5:23) and partially expressing now.

**LXX usage:** *teleios* translates the Hebrew *tāmîm* — wholehearted, blameless, complete before God (Gen 6:9 — Noah was *tāmîm* before God; Deut 18:13 — *"thou shalt be perfect [tāmîm] with the LORD thy God"*). The OT usage is consistently about undivided loyalty and wholehearted orientation toward God — not sinless perfection. Jesus is using the word in its full biblical range.

### 💡 Advanced Hermeneutical Tip — The Crisis-Resolution Arc in Three Texts

The most powerful macro-hermeneutical pattern across these three passages is the **Crisis-Resolution Arc** — a structural pattern that runs through the entire biblical narrative and appears here in compressed form:

| Stage | Passage | Move |
|-------|---------|------|
| **Standard revealed** | Matt 5:1–48 | The law's true depth — heart-level righteousness — is exposed. The Beatitudes describe the citizens of the Kingdom; the antitheses describe their inner life. |
| **Crisis created** | Matt 5:48 | *"Be ye therefore perfect"* — the standard exceeds what human effort can achieve. This is intentional: the sermon ends with an impossible command to drive the hearer to their knees. |
| **Diagnosis given** | Mark 7:14–23 | Why the crisis cannot be self-resolved: the factory of sin is the heart itself. External religion addresses the wrong location entirely. |
| **Resolution announced** | 1 Thess 4:7–8 | God has called you (the indicative), given you His Spirit (the enabler), and is coming back (the completion). The impossible standard is met by a divine worker, not a human achiever. |
| **Completion promised** | 1 Thess 5:23; 1 John 3:2 | *"We shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is."* The *teleios* of Matt 5:48 is fully realized at glorification. |

**Preaching application:** Structure your teaching on holiness to follow this arc. Never give the commands (1 Thess 4) without first establishing the standard (Matt 5) and the diagnosis (Mark 7) — otherwise the commands float free of their theological ground and become moralism. And never leave the arc at the diagnosis — always land on the resolution and the eschatological completion.

### 🔧 Research Workflow — Sermon/Teaching Prep on This Three-Way Correlation

1. **Read all three texts in Greek** — Note: *plēroō* (Matt 5:17), *teleios* (Matt 5:48), *ekporeuontai* (Mark 7:21), *katharizōn* (Mark 7:19), *hagiasmos/akatharsia* (1 Thess 4:3,7), *didonta* (1 Thess 4:8 — present participle). These verbal choices carry the theological weight.
2. **Trace *katharos/koinos/akatharsia*** across all three passages and into Acts 10 and 1 Tim 4 — the vocabulary forms a single semantic network
3. **Commentary stack** — Matt 5: D.A. Carson (*Sermon on the Mount*); Mark 7: William Lane (NICNT); 1 Thess 4: F.F. Bruce (WBC). Read in passage order, then synthesize.
4. **OT backbone** — Read Jer 31:31–34 + Ezek 36:24–27 before preaching any of these three texts. The new covenant promise is the theological ground of the entire arc.
5. **Systematic check** — Run the arc through Bavinck Vol. 3 (sanctification) and Murray's *Principles of Conduct* to ensure the law/gospel and indicative/imperative distinctions are clean before you teach.

### 🎯 Teaching Angle — Teaching Tier 2 + Top 3 Tough Questions

**Framework for Tier 2:** Use the **Crisis-Resolution Arc** as your teaching spine:
- *"Jesus created a crisis in Matthew 5 — the standard is impossibly high"*
- *"Jesus explained the crisis in Mark 7 — the source is irreparably broken"*
- *"Paul announced the resolution in 1 Thessalonians 4 — God provides what He demands"*

**Top 3 Tough Questions:**

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| *"If Matthew 5:48 says 'be perfect,' does that mean we can achieve sinless perfection?"* | No — *teleios* means *complete/whole toward its telos*, not sinless. Phil 3:12 shows Paul denying he has *already* been perfected (*teleiōmai*) while pressing toward it. The standard of Matt 5:48 is the *eschatological destination* we are progressively moving toward, finally complete at glorification (1 John 3:2). It is simultaneously the benchmark and the horizon. |
| *"If the heart produces all those sins in Mark 7, how can I trust any of my desires or motivations?"* | This is the right question — and the answer is discernment, not despair. The regenerate heart is *new* (Ezek 36:26) but not yet *complete* (Phil 3:12). The Spirit produces desires that conflict with the flesh (Gal 5:17). The evidence of regeneration is not the absence of sinful desire but the presence of *warfare against it* (Rom 7:21–25). Distrust desires that bypass the Spirit and the Word; trust desires that are confirmed by them. |
| *"Paul says the Spirit is given — so why do I still struggle so much?"* | Because progressive sanctification is real but not instant. The Spirit's work is not the eradication of the sinful nature in this life but its progressive *mortification* (Rom 8:13) and the simultaneous production of fruit (Gal 5:22–23). The struggle itself — the fact that you grieve over sin and fight it — is evidence of the Spirit's presence, not His absence. A corpse doesn't struggle. |
