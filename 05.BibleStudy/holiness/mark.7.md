## ✝️ CHRISTOLOGICAL ROOT
*The foundation before the study begins*

### 🔎 What Jesus Said/Did

> *"There is nothing from without a man, that entering into him can defile him: but the things which come out of him, those are they that defile the man."* — Mark 7:15 (KJV)

> *"For from within, out of the heart of men, proceed evil thoughts, adulteries, fornications, murders, thefts, covetousness, wickedness, deceit, lasciviousness, an evil eye, blasphemy, pride, foolishness: All these evil things come from within, and defile the man."* — Mark 7:21–23 (KJV)

And Mark's editorial comment on Jesus' full intent:

> *"Because it entereth not into his heart, but into the belly, and goeth out into the draught, purging all meats?"* — Mark 7:19 (KJV)

### 🔗 The Bridge

Jesus does three explosive things in Mark 7 that become the foundation for the entire New Testament theology of holiness, sanctification, and the new covenant. First, He relocates the *source* of defilement from outside a person to inside — the heart is the problem, not the menu or the ritual. Second, by declaring all foods clean, He dismantles the external purity system as the *mechanism* of holiness — not abolishing holiness, but revolutionizing how it is produced. Third, He implicitly announces that the new covenant promised in Ezekiel 36 — *"a new heart will I give you"* — has arrived in His person. Paul unpacks this root in Romans 8:13, 2 Corinthians 3:18, and 1 Timothy 4:5 — the Spirit-enabled inside-out transformation that Jesus diagnosed as the only real solution.

### 📌 Core Concept

Have you ever met someone who looked really good on the outside — said the right words, followed all the rules — but something just felt off? Jesus meets people like that in Mark 7. What comes out of a person — their words, attitudes, choices — that's what shows who they really are. And the only fix for a bad heart is Jesus Himself.

### 🪞 Analogy

Imagine a water filtration system. You need a new source — and that's exactly what the new covenant provides: a new heart (Ezek 36:26).

## 🟡 TIER 2 — THE BUILDER
*Growing believers, small group leaders, Sunday school teachers*

---

### 📌 Deeper Dive

Here's what most people miss about Mark 7: Jesus isn't just correcting bad manners about handwashing — He is dismantling an *entire religious operating system* and replacing it with something the Pharisees couldn't see coming. The stakes are that high.

Mark writes to a predominantly Gentile Roman audience, likely in Rome in the late 50s–60s AD. His Gospel is fast-paced — the word *"straightway"* (immediately) appears over 40 times. He has no interest in long theological speeches. But in chapter 7, he slows down deliberately, because what Jesus says here is architecturally foundational: it redefines where holiness comes from, who can receive it, and what blocks it.

The Pharisees and scribes who confront Jesus in v. 1 have come *from Jerusalem* — the religious capital. This is an official delegation, not a casual encounter. Their charge is about *korban* — the tradition of declaring property dedicated to God as a way of legally avoiding the duty to support aging parents (vv. 10–12). Jesus quotes Isaiah 29:13 back at them: their worship is hollow because they've replaced God's commands with human tradition. The indictment is devastating: the very people most committed to external religion are the ones most effectively nullifying God's Word. Then Jesus turns to the crowd and drops the theological bomb of v. 15 — and Mark makes sure his Gentile readers don't miss the implication by adding his editorial note in v. 19: all foods are clean.

The chapter then pivots to two miracle stories that demonstrate the point. A Gentile woman — doubly disqualified by gender and ethnicity in the religious culture — gets a miracle through sheer, persistent faith (vv. 24–30). A deaf-mute man is healed with a touch and a word: *"Ephphatha — Be opened"* (v. 34). In a chapter about what truly defiles and what truly cleanses, the signs speak loudly: Jesus opens what religion closes, and reaches those religion excludes.

### 🔤 Original Language Insight — *Koinos / Koinoō* (Common / Defile)

**Word:** *κοινός / κοινόω* — **koinos / koinoō**
**Transliteration:** koy-NOS / koy-NO-oh
**Definition:** *Koinos* = common, ordinary, shared — in Jewish ritual context, *ceremonially impure*, *profane*, unfit for sacred use. *Koinoō* = to make common, to defile, to render unclean.

**The word picture:** In Greco-Roman culture, *koinos* simply meant *shared* or *common* — neutral. But in Jewish purity theology it had become the technical term for ritual defilement: what was *koinos* was unfit to approach the holy. The Pharisees' entire handwashing tradition was about preventing *koinosis* — being made common by contact with the unclean world.

**Jesus' radical move:** By declaring that nothing entering from outside *koinoō's* a man (v. 15), Jesus is not rejecting holiness — He is relocating its source. The problem is not external *koinonia* (contact with the common world). The problem is the *koinos* heart — a heart that is itself the source of defilement. Only a new heart — not ritual washing — solves a *koinos* heart.

**In Acts 10:14–15:** Peter uses *koinos* twice in his protest (*"I have never eaten any thing that is common or unclean"*) — and God replies *"What God hath cleansed, that call not thou common."* This is Mark 7:19 applied directly to the Gentile mission. The chapter is not just about food; it is about *who* can be made holy, and *how*.

---

### ⚠️ Common Misconception

**Myth:** *"Jesus abolished the law in Mark 7 — dietary laws, purity codes, all of it is gone, so Christians can basically do whatever they want with their bodies."*

**Truth:** Jesus does not abolish the *purpose* of the purity laws — He fulfills it. The entire Levitical system was a curriculum pointing toward the need for internal purity. Jesus is not saying holiness doesn't matter; He is saying *external performance cannot produce internal purity.* The solution is not license but *transformation*. Paul draws the right conclusion in Romans 8:13 — the same heart Jesus diagnoses in Mark 7 must be actively mortified *through the Spirit.* Jesus ends Pharisaical externalism; He does not end the call to holiness.

## 🔴 TIER 3 — THE MASTERCLASS
*Pastors, teachers, theology students, deep divers*

---

### 📌 Theological Framework

Mark 7 sits at the intersection of four major theological loci:

**1. Hamartiology (The Doctrine of Sin)** — vv. 14–23
Jesus provides the most clinically precise definition of sin's *origin* in the Synoptic Gospels. The heart (*kardia*) is not merely the seat of emotions — in the biblical-Hebraic anthropology it is the center of will, reason, and desire. Jesus' list of twelve vices (vv. 21–22) is not random; it moves from internal dispositions (*evil thoughts, covetousness, pride*) to external acts (*murders, thefts, fornications*), demonstrating that behaviors are downstream of the heart's orientation. This maps directly onto Paul's hamartiology in Romans 1:21–32, where *darkened heart* precedes the vice list, and Genesis 6:5, where every *imagination* of the heart is described as *only evil continually.*

**2. New Covenant Theology** — vv. 15, 19
By declaring all foods clean, Jesus does not abolish the Mosaic covenant — He fulfills its *pedagogical purpose* (Gal 3:24 — the law as *paidagōgos*, a tutor). The entire external purity system was a shadow curriculum teaching Israel that holiness matters and defilement is real. Jesus now reveals the *substance* behind the shadow: the locus of defilement is the heart, and the new covenant solution is a new heart (Ezek 36:26–27; Jer 31:31–34). Mark 7 is one of the clearest Gospel texts demonstrating Jesus as the inaugurator of the new covenant, not merely its beneficiary.

**3. Christology — Messianic Authority** — vv. 6–13, 34
Jesus cites Isaiah and Moses with sovereign authority — not as a rabbi citing precedent but as the Lawgiver Himself clarifying the law's intent. His Aramaic word *Ephphatha* in v. 34 is a direct messianic act: Isa 35:5–6 promised that when the God of Israel comes to save, *"the ears of the deaf shall be unstopped."* Mark preserves the Aramaic precisely because the eyewitness memory of Jesus' exact word mattered — this is not symbolic; it is the King speaking creation into new order.

**4. Missiology — The Gentile Inclusion** — vv. 24–30
The Syrophoenician encounter is a controlled messianic preview. Jesus' initial response (*"Let the children first be filled"* — v. 27) reflects the salvation-historical priority of Israel (Rom 1:16 — *"to the Jew first"*) without excluding the Gentiles. The woman's response (*"the dogs under the table eat of the children's crumbs"* — v. 28) is not groveling — it is a theologically astute counter-argument: she accepts the priority structure while insisting that Gentile access is embedded within it, not excluded by it. Jesus' commendation of her *"saying"* (v. 29) — not just her faith — confirms she has read the messianic script correctly. This encounter becomes the missiological seed that Paul systematizes in Ephesians 2–3 and Romans 9–11.

### 📚 Scholarly Insight

- **William L. Lane** (*NICNT: Mark*, 1974): On vv. 14–23, Lane argues Jesus is performing a *halakic ruling* — the form of rabbinic legal decision — but with unprecedented authority: not *"Rabbi so-and-so says"* but His own declarative. This is the same pattern as the Sermon on the Mount (*"Ye have heard… but I say unto you"*). Jesus functions as the authoritative interpreter of His own Torah.

- **N.T. Wright** (*Jesus and the Victory of God*, 1996): Reads Mark 7 within Jesus' larger critique of the Temple establishment. The purity controversy is not merely about hygiene but about *who constitutes the true covenant people* — Jesus is implicitly redefining Israel around Himself rather than Torah observance, which is why the Pharisees correctly perceive this as a frontal threat.

- **John Calvin** (*Harmony of the Gospels*): On the Syrophoenician woman — Calvin notes that Jesus' apparent harshness is *pedagogical*, not punitive: *"He intended to try her faith, that by her example He might show that the grace of God was not confined to the nation of Israel."* Calvin sees this encounter as a controlled revelation of the Gentile inclusion built into the Abrahamic covenant from the start.

- **Joel Marcus** (*Anchor Yale Bible: Mark 1–8*, 2000): On *koinos/koinoō* — demonstrates that the purity controversy maps onto a first-century Jewish debate about *haver* (associate) vs. *am ha-aretz* (people of the land) purity — the Pharisees were enforcing table-fellowship restrictions designed for priests on all Israel. Jesus demolishes the category entirely.

### ⚔️ Debate Corner — Three Contested Issues in Mark 7

**1. Did Jesus Abolish the Mosaic Law in Mark 7:19?**

*Abolitionist reading:* Jesus' declaration (*"purging all meats"*) represents a clean break with the Mosaic food laws — the entire ceremonial law is cancelled. Common in dispensational frameworks.

*Fulfillment reading (Reformed/Historic):* Jesus does not abolish but fulfills (Matt 5:17). The food laws were pedagogical — teaching the principle of holiness and separation. Jesus reveals the principle's true target (the heart) and declares the external curriculum completed in His person. The law's *moral core* remains; its *ceremonial shadow* is fulfilled and therefore superseded.

*New Perspective reading (N.T. Wright, Dunn):* The food laws functioned primarily as *social boundary markers* separating Jew from Gentile. Jesus' declaration removes the *ethnic boundary function* of Torah, enabling Gentile inclusion — but does not eliminate holiness as a category.

**Reformed assessment:** The fulfillment reading is most consistent with the canonical witness. Matthew 5:17–19 is the interpretive control: Jesus *fulfills*, not abolishes. The threefold law distinction (moral/ceremonial/civil) — while not explicit in Scripture — provides a useful framework: the moral law (expressed in the Decalogue and its NT applications) stands; the ceremonial law is fulfilled in Christ; the civil law's application is adapted to the new covenant context.

**2. Was the Syrophoenician Woman's Faith an Exception or a Preview?**

*Exceptional case view:* Jesus crossed a boundary uniquely for this woman due to her exceptional faith — this is not meant to be programmatic.

*Missiological preview view (Reformed/majority):* The encounter is deliberately structured by Mark as a preview of the Gentile mission that Acts will narrate. The woman's correct reading of the messianic framework, combined with Jesus' commendation, establishes a theological principle: Gentile faith that grasps the salvation-historical structure has genuine access to messianic grace. This reading is confirmed by Mark's placement of the story immediately after the food purity declaration — both texts are about who can be clean and who can receive.

**Reformed assessment:** The missiological preview view is exegetically stronger and canonically coherent — confirmed by Acts 10, Romans 9–11, and Ephesians 2–3.

**3. Is Mark 7:34 (*Ephphatha*) Evidence of Jesus' Divine Authority or a Healing Formula?**

*Formula view:* Some early critics argued Jesus used a magical formula common in the ancient world — the preserved Aramaic reflects a healing incantation.

*Sovereign command view (mainstream evangelical/Reformed):* The Aramaic is preserved because the eyewitness memory was precise, not because it functions as a formula. Jesus speaks to physical organs as He speaks to storms (Mark 4:39), the dead (John 11:43), and demons (Mark 1:25) — with the authority of the Creator addressing His creation. The Isaiah 35 background confirms this is a messianic sovereign act, not a technique.

**Reformed assessment:** The sovereign command view is the only reading consistent with Mark's high Christology throughout the Gospel and the explicit Isaiah 35 messianic backdrop.

⚠️ **Pastoral Warning:** The debate over whether Jesus abolished the law (Issue 1) has produced real antinomian casualties in churches that read Mark 7:19 as a general license. Always teach the abolition/fulfillment distinction carefully — the ceremonial is done; the moral demand remains, now written on the heart by the Spirit.

### 🔤 Original Language Deep Dive — *Kardia* (Heart)

**Word:** *καρδία* — **kardia**
**Parsing:** Noun, feminine — used 52 times in Mark's Gospel context across the NT
**Definition:** In NT anthropology, *kardia* is not primarily the seat of emotions (as in modern English) — it is the *center of the person's being*: the seat of will, intellect, moral orientation, and spiritual life

**OT Hebrew background:** *lēb / lēbāb* (לֵב / לֵבָב) — used over 850 times in the OT. Translated *kardia* in the LXX. The Hebrew heart is the place of:
- *Cognition* — Prov 23:7 — *"as he thinketh in his heart, so is he"*
- *Volition* — Exod 35:5 — *"whosoever is of a willing heart"*
- *Moral orientation* — Ps 51:10 — *"Create in me a clean heart, O God"*
- *Spiritual receptivity* — Deut 6:5 — *"thou shalt love the LORD thy God with all thine heart"*

**In Mark 7:** Jesus locates the entire list of vv. 21–22 as proceeding *ek tēs kardias* — **out of the heart**. This is the most comprehensive statement about sin's origin in the Gospels. Every vice — from murder to foolishness — has its generating source in the heart's orientation. This makes the new covenant's promise of a *new heart* (Ezek 36:26) not merely a moral upgrade but an ontological necessity.

**LXX significance:** The LXX's consistent translation of *lēb* as *kardia* means the NT writers and their audiences understood *kardia* through the full weight of the OT's anthropological framework — not the reduced Greek sense of merely emotional center. When Jesus says *"out of the heart,"* He is invoking the entire OT diagnosis of human nature.

### 💡 Advanced Hermeneutical Tip — Chiastic Structure in Mark 7

Mark 7 exhibits a **chiastic structure** that reveals its theological center:

```
A  — Pharisees challenge Jesus on tradition (vv. 1–5)
  B  — Jesus quotes Isaiah: lips vs. heart (vv. 6–8)
    C  — Korban: tradition nullifies God's command (vv. 9–13)
      D  — THE PIVOT: Nothing outside defiles; heart is the source (vv. 14–23)
    C' — Syrophoenician: faith overrides ethnic tradition (vv. 24–30)
  B' — Deaf-mute healed: Jesus opens ears and mouths (vv. 31–37)
A' — (implied) New definition of purity established
```

The center (D) is the theological heart of the chapter: the relocation of defilement from external to internal. Everything before it (A–C) sets up the problem; everything after it (C'–B') demonstrates the solution. Teaching this structure helps your congregation see that the miracles in vv. 24–37 are not appendices — they are *embodied answers* to the theological declaration of vv. 14–23.

### 🎯 Teaching Angle — Teaching Tier 2 + Top 3 Tough Questions

**Framework:** *"The Outside-In vs. Inside-Out Religion"*
- Section 1: The Pharisees built an *outside-in* religion — control the external, and holiness results
- Section 2: Jesus diagnoses the fatal flaw — the factory is internal; you cannot scrub your way to holiness
- Section 3: The two miracles show Jesus doing what outside-in religion never could — reaching the excluded and opening the closed

**Top 3 Tough Questions:**

| Question | Answer |
|----------|--------|
| *"If Jesus declared all foods clean, why did Peter need the Acts 10 vision?"* | Because the disciples' understanding of Mark 7's implications was incomplete until Pentecost and the Spirit's guidance (John 16:13 — *"he will guide you into all truth"*). The Acts 10 vision is not a correction of Mark 7 — it is the Spirit applying Mark 7 to the concrete situation of Gentile inclusion. Peter needed the vision because his cultural formation ran deeper than his theological understanding. |
| *"Was Jesus being cruel to the Syrophoenician woman?"* | No — He was testing and teaching simultaneously. Calvin is right: Jesus was drawing out her faith in order to commend it publicly. The *"children and dogs"* language, while sharp, was a rhetorical setup she accepted and turned — demonstrating genuine theological comprehension. Her persistence and her argument reveal a faith that understood the messianic framework better than the Pharisees who had the credentials. |
| *"Does Mark 7 mean our heart is so corrupt that trying to do good is useless?"* | The diagnosis of the heart (vv. 21–23) describes the *unregenerate* heart's output — it does not mean regenerate believers cannot resist sin. The entire point of the new covenant (Ezek 36:26–27) is that God replaces the stone heart with a flesh heart and puts His Spirit within — enabling real transformation. Mark 7 shows the *problem*; the new covenant and the Spirit provide the *solution*. Romans 8 is the full answer. |
