# PART 1 — GEO FUNDAMENTALS

## What GEO Is and Why It's Completely Different From SEO

Traditional SEO optimizes for Google to list your website as a link.

GEO optimizes for AI systems to **cite you as the answer.**

The difference is not subtle. When someone searches Google for "best buyer's agent in Park Slope Brooklyn," they get a list of links they must evaluate and click. When someone asks ChatGPT or Perplexity the same question, they get a direct answer — often naming specific agents, with a brief explanation of why. The user doesn't browse. They trust.

**The conversion implication:** AI-referred visitors convert at approximately 4.4× the rate of organic search visitors. The AI pre-qualifies the recommendation. By the time someone calls an agent that ChatGPT cited, they've already been told this person is the expert. The sales conversation starts differently.

**The competitive implication:** As of 2024–2025, virtually no NYC real estate agent has optimized for AI search. The traditional SEO game is crowded — StreetEasy, Zillow, Compass, and Corcoran have spent years and millions building Google authority. GEO is a new game, and the scoreboard is nearly blank.

The first agents to establish AI search citability in their neighborhoods will hold that position as AI search grows — just as the first agents to build strong Google SEO in 2010 still dominate today.

**NYC real estate relevance:** Extremely high — users ask detailed, specific questions and expect sourced answers

### 3. Google AI Overviews
**User base:** Everyone who uses Google — the largest potential audience
**How it sources:** Googlebot index — your existing Google rankings heavily influence AI Overview citations
**Citation style:** Appears at the top of Google results for informational queries — summarizes with 3–5 source links
**NYC real estate relevance:** Highest traffic volume — Google AI Overviews now appear for the majority of real estate informational queries
**Key query example:** "How does co-op board approval work in NYC?"

### 4. Bing Copilot (Microsoft)
**User base:** Microsoft 365 users, enterprise professionals, LinkedIn users
**How it sources:** Bing index (Bingbot) + Microsoft's AI layer — strong LinkedIn integration
**Citation style:** Conversational with source links — heavily weights LinkedIn content and Microsoft ecosystem

**User base:** Professional, technical, business users — growing rapidly

# PART 2 — THE CITABILITY FRAMEWORK

## What Makes Content Citable by AI Systems vs. Ignored

AI systems don't cite randomly. They extract and attribute content that meets specific quality signals. Understanding these signals is the core of GEO strategy.

Think of an AI system as an extremely well-read researcher who must cite their sources. They will cite content that is: clearly authoritative, factually dense, directly answering the question, and attributable to a credible named source. They will not cite content that is thin, vague, uncredentialed, or blocked from indexing.

---

## The 7 Citability Signals

### Signal 1: Topical Authority Depth (Weight: Critical)
An AI system cites sources that have demonstrated extensive, consistent coverage of a topic. One excellent article about Park Slope real estate is less citable than a website with 20 deep, specific articles about Park Slope real estate.

**What this means:** Build content breadth in your specific neighborhoods before going broad. Own the depth on 3 neighborhoods before expanding to 10.

**Scoring:** Rate 1–10 — how many pages of deep, specific content exist for each target neighborhood/topic?

### Signal 2: E-E-A-T Density (Weight: Critical)
Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness — these signals tell AI systems that a source is qualified to be cited. Unlike Google's interpretation (which relies heavily on backlinks), AI systems evaluate E-E-A-T through content signals: specific credentials stated, transaction history referenced, years of experience documented, real outcomes described.

**What this means:** Every page of your site should contain credentialing signals — not just your About page.

**Scoring:** Rate 1–10 — how many pages contain explicit E-E-A-T signals?

### Signal 3: Answer-First Content Structure (Weight: High)
AI systems extract direct answers. Content that buries the answer after three paragraphs of preamble is harder to cite than content that leads with the answer.

**What this means:** Restructure every section to answer the implicit question in the first sentence.

**Scoring:** Rate 1–10 — what percentage of sections lead with a direct, citable answer?

### Signal 4: Factual Density (Weight: High)
Specific numbers, verifiable dates, named places, quantified outcomes — these are what AI systems extract and cite. Vague, general content ("the co-op process can take a while") is not citable. Specific content ("co-op board approvals in NYC typically take 4–8 weeks after submission of the board package") is citable.

**What this means:** Replace every vague claim with a specific, verifiable one.

**Scoring:** Rate 1–10 — what percentage of claims include specific, verifiable data?

### Signal 5: Third-Party Brand Mentions (Weight: High)
AI systems weight sources that appear across multiple authoritative platforms. An agent mentioned only on their own website is less authoritative than an agent mentioned on their website, Zillow, StreetEasy, REBNY directory, LinkedIn, Brick Underground, and the NY Post real estate section.

**What this means:** Your brand mention footprint across authoritative third-party platforms is a GEO asset.

**Scoring:** Rate 1–10 — how many authoritative platforms mention this agent by name?

### Signal 6: Structured Data and Schema Markup (Weight: Medium-High)
JSON-LD schema markup tells AI systems precisely what content represents — who this person is, what they do, where they operate, what credentials they hold. Without schema, AI systems must infer. With schema, you tell them directly.

**What this means:** Every key page should have relevant schema markup deployed correctly.

**Scoring:** Rate 1–10 — how complete and accurate is the schema markup across the site?

### Signal 7: Crawl Accessibility (Weight: Foundational)
None of the above matters if AI crawlers can't access your content. A robots.txt file blocking GPTBot, ClaudeBot, or PerplexityBot makes your site invisible to those AI systems regardless of content quality.

**What this means:** Check robots.txt first. Fix any AI crawler blocks before doing anything else.

**Scoring:** Rate 1–10 — are all major AI crawlers permitted? Is there an llms.txt file?

## How to Check Your robots.txt Right Now

Navigate to: `[yourwebsite.com]/robots.txt`

A healthy robots.txt for GEO visibility looks like this:

```
User-agent: *
Allow: /

User-agent: Googlebot
Allow: /

User-agent: GPTBot
Allow: /

User-agent: ClaudeBot
Allow: /

User-agent: PerplexityBot
Allow: /

User-agent: bingbot
Allow: /

Sitemap: https://[yourwebsite.com]/sitemap.xml
```

**Red flags to look for:**
- `User-agent: * Disallow: /` — blocks everything including AI crawlers
- `User-agent: GPTBot Disallow: /` — explicitly blocks ChatGPT
- Any `Disallow` directive that would block your content pages
- Missing sitemap reference

Many WordPress themes and hosting providers add restrictive robots.txt rules by default. Check yours today.

# PART 4 — LLMS.TXT: THE AI WELCOME MAT

## What llms.txt Is and Why It's Your GEO First-Mover Advantage

The `llms.txt` file is an emerging standard that allows website owners to provide AI language models with structured information about their site — who they are, what content exists, what should be prioritized for AI consumption.

Think of it as: robots.txt tells crawlers what they *can't* access. llms.txt tells AI systems what they *should* read first and how to understand it.

**Location:** `[yourwebsite.com]/llms.txt`

**Why it matters right now:** Almost no NYC real estate agent has one. An AI system encountering a site with a well-structured llms.txt immediately has a clearer understanding of that agent's authority and content than any competitor without one. It's the digital equivalent of walking into a meeting with a perfectly prepared briefing document while your competitors wing it.

**The format:** Markdown-based. Structured to introduce the site owner, list key pages with descriptions, and guide AI systems toward your most authoritative content.

# PART 5 — E-E-A-T FOR AI SYSTEMS

## How AI Systems Evaluate E-E-A-T Differently Than Google

Google's E-E-A-T evaluation relies heavily on: inbound links from authoritative sites, author markup, Google's quality rater guidelines, and historical domain signals.

AI systems evaluate E-E-A-T primarily through **content signals** — what the content itself says about the author's qualifications, experience, and authority. This is both a challenge and an opportunity: you can directly influence AI E-E-A-T signals by what you write, not just who links to you.

**The AI Trust Stack for NYC Real Estate:**

| Signal | What AI Systems Look For | How to Provide It |
|--------|------------------------|------------------|
| License Verification | NY State license number, verifiable | State it explicitly on every author bio |
| Transaction History | Specific deals, neighborhoods, outcomes | Reference real transactions (with permission) |
| Neighborhood Knowledge | Street-level specificity, local facts | Demonstrate in content — not just claim |
| Third-Party Presence | Mentions on authoritative platforms | Build the brand mention ecosystem |
| Association Membership | REBNY, NAR, local board membership | State explicitly, link to membership profile |
| Client Outcomes | Specific results — "above asking," "30-day close" | Testimonials with specifics |
| Market Data Knowledge | Original analysis, specific stats | Create and publish your own data |
| Professional Network | Who you work with, who references you | Cross-linking, co-authored content |

### The sameAs Property: Your Identity Graph

The `sameAs` property is the most powerful GEO signal in schema markup. It tells AI systems: "All of these profiles are the same person." This creates a unified identity that AI systems can confidently cite — because multiple authoritative sources confirm the same identity.

Every profile you create on every platform should be referenced in the `sameAs` array. The more authoritative the platform (LinkedIn, NY State registry, REBNY directory), the stronger the identity confirmation.

# PART 8 — ANSWER-ENGINE CONTENT OPTIMIZATION

## The Structural Difference Between SEO Content and AI-Citable Content

**SEO content structure (traditional):**
Introduction → Background → Detail → More Detail → Conclusion → Answer

**AI-citable content structure (answer-engine):**
Direct Answer → Supporting Evidence → NYC-Specific Context → Practical Application

AI systems extract answers. They do not read entire articles and summarize them the way a human would. They find the most direct, clear, authoritative statement that answers the query — and they extract it. If your answer is buried in paragraph four, they may never get to it, or may cite a competitor whose answer appears in paragraph one.

---

## The 5 Content Formats AI Systems Cite Most

**1. Direct Question + Direct Answer (FAQ format)**
Best for: Process questions, definition questions, cost questions
AI citation probability: Very High
Example: "What is a co-op board package in NYC?" → [Direct 2-sentence answer]

**2. Step-by-Step Process Guides**
Best for: Transaction process, application processes, timelines
AI citation probability: High
Example: "How to buy a co-op in NYC" → numbered steps with specific timeframes

**3. Comparison Content**
Best for: Decision-making queries
AI citation probability: High
Example: "Co-op vs. condo NYC" → comparison table + direct recommendation

**4. Statistical/Data Content**
Best for: Market questions, cost questions, timeline questions
AI citation probability: Very High when original data
Example: "Average days on market in Park Slope" → specific number from your data

**5. Expert Commentary**
Best for: "What should I know about..." queries
AI citation probability: High when credentials are established
Example: "What do I need to know about buying in Astoria in 2025?"

## AI Invisibility Traps to Avoid

**Trap 1: Blocking AI Crawlers by Default**
Website platforms (Squarespace, some WordPress setups, Wix) and security plugins (Cloudflare, various WAF configurations) sometimes block AI crawlers by default — without you knowing. Check your robots.txt at [yoursite.com/robots.txt] today. Check your Cloudflare or CDN settings for any bot-blocking rules. Check your WordPress security plugin settings. This is the single most common and most easily fixable GEO problem — and many agents have it without realizing it.

**Trap 2: Generic Content That Says Nothing Specific**
"Park Slope is a wonderful neighborhood with a vibrant community and excellent schools" is not citable. It contains no specific information an AI system would extract to answer a factual question. Every sentence of your content should contain information — not just impressions. Replace every descriptive vague sentence with a specific one. "Park Slope's PS 321 is consistently ranked among Brooklyn's top elementary schools, serving families in a zone that covers approximately 10 blocks east of Prospect Park" is citable.

**Trap 3: E-E-A-T Buried on the About Page**
Most agents put their credentials on the About page and nowhere else. AI systems reading your Park Slope neighborhood guide find no credentials — they have no way to attribute the content to an authoritative source. Weave credentials into every content page. Every neighborhood guide, every market report, every blog post should contain the author's name, license reference, and relevant experience statement. "According to [Agent Name], a licensed NYC real estate agent (NY License #XXXXXX) with 12 years of co-op transaction experience in Park Slope..." establishes citability on every page.

**Trap 4: Inconsistent NAP Across Platforms**
If your name appears as "John Smith" on your website, "John A. Smith" on Zillow, "J. Smith" on StreetEasy, and "John Smith, Realtor" on LinkedIn — AI systems may treat these as four separate entities rather than one authoritative identity. The identity graph requires consistency. Use the exact same name format, address, and phone number across every platform. The `sameAs` schema property then confirms that all of these are the same person.

**Trap 5: Publishing Without Schema Markup**
Every piece of content published without schema markup is a missed GEO opportunity. Schema markup tells AI systems precisely what the content is, who wrote it, and what authority it carries. It takes 15 minutes to add Article schema to a blog post, FAQPage schema to an FAQ section, or RealEstateAgent schema to an About page. The cumulative effect of schema markup across an entire site is significant. Set up schema templates once and apply them to every new piece of content going forward.

**Trap 6: Measuring GEO With SEO Tools**
Ahrefs, SEMrush, and Moz don't measure AI search visibility. They measure Google rankings. You can have excellent Google rankings and zero GEO visibility — or modest Google rankings and strong AI citation. Measure GEO separately: manually test your target queries on each AI platform monthly, track brand mentions across platforms, and monitor your GEO scorecard. The tools don't exist yet for full GEO measurement automation — manual testing is the current standard.

**Trap 7: Optimizing for One AI Platform**
Optimizing only for ChatGPT visibility ignores Perplexity (the highest-citation-density platform), Google AI Overviews (the highest-traffic platform), and Bing Copilot (the LinkedIn-integrated platform). Each platform has different sourcing behaviors and different citation criteria. The full GEO system optimizes for the common denominators (answer-first content, E-E-A-T, schema, brand mentions) that benefit all platforms — and then addresses platform-specific tactics for the ones most relevant to your target clientele.
